1Introduction

Mr. G.M.B. Kumara, age ~40s (exact figure unclear in scan), farmer from place name unclear, who lives with his younger son in their own home. History was taken from the patient and collateral history from his elder son. He was transferred from another ward, 1 day back.

2Presenting Complaint

Tingling sensation in right side lower limb for 8 months, headache for 5 days, and a palpable lump in the tongue for 4 months.

3History of Presenting Complaint

He is a known patient with a psychiatric illness since 2019, but defaulted clinic follow-up. This time he presented to the ward due to multiple complaints. According to the elder son, the patient refused to go to the local hospital and insisted on admission at THA.

He has gradually worsening numbness below knee level in the lower limb, which he describes as a "pins and needles" type sensation. He had taken medication for this at the local hospital, but did not take it properly, as he firmly believes that someone is changing his medications in order to harm him.

He has had headache for 5 days duration — bilateral, frontal. He claims it only occurs while he is inside the home, and is relieved when he goes outside. He firmly believes that a group of known people have put some toxic gas inside the home in order to sedate and harm him.

He also has a small, painless, palpable lump in the middle of his tongue for 4 months duration. He believes this to be a small microphone, inserted by the same group of people, to hear what he is saying and talk about him.

Before this time he never sought medical attention for complaints other than these. His suspicion began when he started to doubt his wife regarding an extramarital relationship, firmly believing that his wife poisoned him through home-made food, along with a burning-type epigastric pain.

He believes his wife has an extramarital relationship, and that a group of known people have sexually abused her and are blackmailing her, as they have videos of his wife. He believes his wife is trying to harm him, and that these people injected him with a virus after sedating him with gas. Even though he complained of these symptoms to a medical officer, he did not get a satisfactory response, so he believes the medical officer is also part of this conspiracy. He has undergone several blood investigations, and all reports came back normal.

He firmly believes the group of people put toxic gas into his clothes and bed linen, making it difficult for him to breathe, so he washes his clothes repeatedly before wearing them. According to his son, his symptoms have now worsened, and he wears freshly washed, still-wet clothes in the mornings.

Other than the microphone, he claims the group of people have installed cameras inside his home to monitor what he is doing. He claims that during the night, people give signals to his wife — such as breaking sticks outside the home — and when his wife coughs, he believes she is signalling them too.

His wife is now separated from him, but he still believes the group is controlling her to poison him, so he does not eat anywhere except at one of his brothers' houses.

According to his elder son, the patient has now locked every door in their home, closed all windows, and sealed them with cellotape. Most of the time he does not sleep at night, and instead goes gambling with friends, returning around 4–5am. He has spent a lot of money on this, and has ultimately sold most of his belongings, including a motorbike and a tractor, and now has loans.

His son also says that sometimes the patient does not speak even to him, and instead gives written letters, as the patient believes someone is listening through the microphone in his tongue. On some days the patient has beaten his wife badly while questioning her about the extramarital relationships.

According to the patient, in 2013 he stabbed one of the villagers following a paddy land dispute. He was then placed on oral medication, as he worried excessively about the incident, and was on treatment for 6 months. Since then, until 2019, he did not have any problems. His suspiciousness regarding his wife started to develop from 2014.

Before that, he was doing his job happily, with no financial issues, and was a loving husband and father to his wife and 2 sons. Even with this illness, he still does his job properly, but now has significant financial issues and several loans, and is asking his elder son to help pay them off — though his son does not have that much money either.

The patient believes his thoughts are his own, and no external force has inserted or withdrawn them. He denies that his thoughts are known to others without him communicating them. He occasionally consumes alcohol and smokes, with no history of any other psychoactive substance misuse.

He denies feelings of excessive sadness, low mood, fatigue, or loss of interest. He denies early morning headache, vomiting, or head trauma. He does not have any suicidal thoughts or ideas.

He does have a history of violence — the stabbing incident in 2013 — and does not have intention to harm his current wife, but he does not care much about his younger son's schooling, as he believes the group of people are watching him. He has good self-care.

Persistent delusional disorder — core diagnostic features

The Shorter Oxford Textbook defines persistent (delusional) disorder as the development of a single delusion, or a set of related delusions, that are usually persistent and sometimes lifelong, in the absence of prominent or sustained hallucinations, thought disorder, or the marked flattening of affect seen in schizophrenia. Personality and daily functioning are typically well preserved outside the area of the delusion — consistent here with the patient continuing his farming work and maintaining otherwise coherent, goal-directed behaviour, while remaining fully systematised around the belief that his wife and a conspiring group are trying to harm him.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Persistent Delusional Disorders.

4Family History

He has 4 brothers & 3 sisters. No first-degree relatives with any psychiatric illness. No family history of suicides or attempts.

5Personal History

No significant events during childhood and adolescence. He was educated up to O/L, then went to work in a garment factory for several years, where he met his wife. He married at the age of ~21 years. He raised 2 sons and lived happily with them & his wife. Now they have separated but not divorced.

Now the patient does paddy farming and lives in their own house with his younger son. His elder son works as a bus conductor in Kandy. His wife is now babysitting elsewhere. Currently he does not have a good companion other than his 2 children.

6Past Psychiatric History

As mentioned above, in 2013 he stabbed one of his neighbours, which led to that person's death, following a paddy land dispute. He was then referred to psychiatric services and treated for 6 months. He worried excessively about the incident and had low mood for a few months; even now, from time to time, he still worries about it.

However, his family and the deceased person's family currently have no ongoing conflicts. According to his son, the patient did not have any abnormal behaviour until 2019, when he started to suspect his wife, believing she went out to meet other people after dropping the children at school.

The patient believes that thereafter his wife was sexually abused by a known group of people, and claims he has video recordings of this. He claims those people blackmailed his wife, and that she started poisoning him since then, with his symptoms gradually worsening over the following years.

The patient does not have a past history of suicidal attempts or self-harm, but does have a history of violence against his wife, according to his son, because of which she has left home. According to the son, the wife is willing to return home, but because of the patient's aggressive behaviour towards her, she couldn't stay more than one day. His son has tried several times to bring the patient to the psychiatric ward, but the patient refused.

7Past Medical / Surgical History

Has had an episode of hypertension, but no records available. Not on any long-term medications.

8Premorbid Personality

Until 2014, he had a good relationship with his wife & 2 sons, and they lived happily. Other than the 2013 incident, he was a very friendly person with his neighbours & relatives. He used to farm and was able to manage his day-to-day activities alone. When he had a problem, he would tell one of his brothers, from whom he felt some relief. He claims that initially his brother listened to him, but later started to ignore him.

He is a religious person, and claims his stress is reduced after praying to Lord Buddha. He spends his leisure time watching TV with friends, and sometimes goes to worship with his son. He tended to easily trust his friends' opinions, believing what they told him without much question. He does not have any evidence of maladaptive coping strategies like self-harm or substance abuse.

★ Risk Assessment

Suicidal risk — Low. He denies a sense of hopelessness, and has no suicidal thoughts or clear plans about suicide. No past attempts of suicide or deliberate self-harm. No family history of psychiatric illness or suicide. He consumes alcohol and smokes only occasionally, with no use of psychoactive substances, which act as protective factors.

However, he currently has no close relationships other than his 2 children, and has ongoing stressors against this background. He has access to pesticides and weapons at home but continuously denies suicidal thoughts. So his suicidal risk is low.

Risk to others — High. He does not have clear plans to harm anyone, but wants to punish the people he believes are blackmailing his wife, and wants to take legal action against them. He meets these people in his day-to-day life; even when he gets angry while talking to them, he does not want to argue.

He has a risk of harming his wife because of his delusion of jealousy and persecutory delusions, and has a past history of violence against her. He also has a past history of violence against another person during a land dispute, where he stabbed a villager. He still has access to weapons at home — he owns knives & machetes. So his risk to others is high.

Risk to children — High. His younger son lives alone with the patient in their home; when the patient is admitted to hospital, there is no one to look after the younger child. The patient has sold most home belongings, including a motorbike and a tractor, and now has loans he is pressuring his elder son to pay. So his risk of neglect of children is high.

Risk of self-neglect — Moderate. Because of his persecutory delusions, he does not eat outside food much, so his food intake is reduced. His sleep at night is also reduced, as he firmly believes people are trying to harm him. He firmly believes toxic gas has been put into his clothes and bed linen, so he wears wet clothes just after washing them, risking skin irritation/infestation. Even though his overall hygiene is good, he has a moderate risk of self-neglect.

Delusional jealousy (Othello syndrome) & risk

The Shorter Oxford Textbook flags delusional jealousy as one of the higher-risk delusional themes, given its strong association with violence towards the (perceived unfaithful) partner, and recommends that risk to the spouse/partner be assessed as carefully as risk to self. This case demonstrates that principle clearly: despite low suicidal risk, the combination of delusional jealousy, systematised persecutory beliefs, and a documented past history of serious violence (the 2013 stabbing) place risk to others — especially the wife — firmly in the high category, and is the central driver of the admission decision.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Persistent Delusional Disorders / Risk Assessment.

3Mood

4Thought

He has delusion of jealousy, firmly believing his wife is having extramarital relationships with a group of known people, who possess videos of her and are blackmailing her — though according to his son there is no such evidence. He also has persecutory delusions, firmly believing the same group and his wife are trying to harm him by poisoning his food and putting toxic gas into his clothes and bed linen, causing breathing difficulty. He also believes they have implanted a microphone in his tongue and installed cameras inside his home to monitor his conversations and activities.

Other than this, he does not have flight of ideas, no thought insertion or withdrawal.

5Perception

6Cognition

He is oriented to time, place and person. Attention & concentration are good, with no long-term or short-term memory impairment.

7Insight

Impaired. Even though other people believe his behaviour is abnormal, he does not accept this himself, and does not believe he has a psychotic illness. However, he is willing to take medications to relieve his beliefs.

Systematised delusion without hallucinations

A hallmark distinguishing persistent delusional disorder from schizophrenia, per the Shorter Oxford Textbook, is the absence of prominent hallucinations and formal thought disorder despite an elaborate, internally logical delusional system (here spanning the microphone, cameras, poisoning, and infidelity beliefs, all woven into one coherent persecutory-jealous narrative). The mood and cognition being otherwise intact and reactive further supports the delusional disorder diagnosis over schizophrenia or an affective psychosis.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Symptoms and Signs of Mental Disorder / Delusional Disorders.

Ruling out schizophrenia and mood psychoses here

The Shorter Oxford Textbook notes that schizophrenia is favoured when Schneiderian first-rank symptoms, marked negative symptoms, or significant functional decline are present — none of which are prominent here, as the patient continues to farm and maintains coherent, non-disorganised speech. Depression with psychosis is excluded by the absence of a pervasive low mood or biological depressive features; his mood is explicitly euthymic. This leaves persistent delusional disorder as the best fit, with the somatic complaints (tongue lump, headache, limb tingling) understood as delusional elaborations rather than separate organic pathology, especially given normal investigations.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Differential Diagnosis of Psychotic Disorders.

Goals of Management

Probable diagnosis: Persistent delusional disorder, jealousy type.

  • Improve & relieve current symptoms
  • Minimise symptoms & their consequences
  • Improve wellbeing of patient and family

Immediate Management

(1) Decide on treatment setting. I recommend managing this patient as an in-ward patient because: he has impaired insight; there is risk of violence against his wife; poor social support; risk of self-neglect; and to reduce his current stressful situation, given his persecutory delusions.

(2) Provide supportive environment. My priority would be to provide a supportive & structured environment for minimising any potential risk to himself & others. I would inform the staff about the patient and advise close monitoring of behaviour, appetite & sleep.

(3) Assess the patient. I would conduct a thorough assessment including detailed history from him & collateral history from his son. Then a thorough physical examination & mental state examination. If available, I would go through his past medical and clinic records to inform the management plan, and complete a full risk assessment including suicidal risk, risk of violence, risk of self-neglect, and risk to children.

I would order baseline investigations including FBC, LFTs, RFTs, lipid profile, ECG, and serum electrolytes.

(4) Educating the patient and family members. I would explain that this is a chronic psychiatric illness, covering aetiologies, symptoms, treatment options, and side effects of available treatments. I would explain why this patient needs in-ward care, and the importance of adherence.

(5) Pharmacological management. After tracing his past medical records, I would start him on second-generation antipsychotics as the mainstay of treatment — e.g. Risperidone, or Olanzapine 5mg nocte — titrating the dose to an effective level as tolerated. If the patient becomes agitated or anxious, I would consider adding a short-acting benzodiazepine temporarily, e.g. Clonazepam 0.5mg nocte. I would also consider a topical antifungal for skin rashes (given the wet-clothes behaviour), and attend to his nutrition & hydration.

Non-pharmacological management. I would build a good rapport with the patient and acknowledge his distress, validating his emotions without reinforcing the delusional content. If there are any acute needs (e.g. childcare arrangements for the younger son), I would address them accordingly.

Talking with a patient about their delusions

The Shorter Oxford Textbook advises against direct confrontation of delusional beliefs, which typically entrenches them further, while also cautioning against colluding with or reinforcing the content. The recommended stance is to validate the patient's distress and acknowledge how real the experience feels to them, build engagement and trust, and work gradually towards gentle reality-testing once rapport is established — exactly the approach outlined in this plan.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Management of Delusional Disorders.

Intermediate Management

I would conduct daily ward rounds to assess his symptoms and potential side effects, medication adherence, and risk. I would monitor for improvement; if response is poor, I would increase the dose, and if there is still no improvement, consider drug combination or switching to another agent with cross-tapering, or seek senior opinion.

I would arrange CBT for delusions, to build insight and support reality testing, and consider family therapy to manage relational discord & reduce expressed emotion within the family.

If there is comorbid depression or OCD, I would address this with antidepressants if required. I would arrange a rehabilitation programme to support his re-integration into work.

I would slowly taper benzodiazepines to prevent dependence. As symptoms improve, I would arrange a parole leave for a few days to a week, and on return assess improvement of symptoms, drug side effects, and any needed dose adjustments.

Long-term Management

Goals: plan for discharge and follow-up care. When symptoms improve, I would prepare a discharge summary including clinical features, treatment, follow-up plan, and key contact person, and arrange follow-up at THA or a base hospital depending on the availability of medications.

At each follow-up visit, I would educate the patient on the importance of treatment adherence and assess compliance, as the main purpose is to maintain the achieved improvement & prevent relapse. I would assess the patient physically, psychologically & socially — including risk, occupational functioning, social support, and drug compliance.

I would continue supportive psychotherapy, working to gently improve his insight and prevent delusional rationalisation without direct confrontation. I would address any relationship issues, and arrange couple counselling.

Family involvement & expressed emotion

Family-focused work is a recurring theme in the Shorter Oxford Textbook's approach to psychotic disorders: reducing "expressed emotion" (hostility, criticism, over-involvement) within the household is associated with lower relapse rates. Here, involving the son as the primary collateral historian and support figure, alongside couple counselling once safe to consider, reflects this principle — while safety planning (for the wife and younger son) must take priority given the documented violence risk.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Family Interventions in Psychosis.

Antipsychotic response in delusional disorder

The Shorter Oxford Textbook notes that response to antipsychotics in persistent delusional disorder is more variable than in schizophrenia — some patients show good symptomatic response, but many retain some encapsulated delusional content even with adherence. This makes the psychosocial components (rapport, gentle insight-building, family work, risk monitoring) just as central to management as medication, which is reflected in the emphasis on non-pharmacological strategies throughout this case.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Persistent Delusional Disorders — Treatment and Prognosis.
