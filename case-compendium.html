CASE 01

Damayanthi, 30F

Recurrent Depressive Disorder, current episode severe, without psychotic symptoms
Background: Mother of 2, transferred from Thabuttegama DH with suicidal ideas. On Fe for anaemia; received 3 ECT sessions this admission. Background of harmful alcohol use by husband, frequent quarrels, domestic violence, husband repeatedly imprisoned. Concerns re: daughters' safety. Physical exam: healed self-harm scars, cannula site infection.
What is your diagnosis?›

Severe depression without psychotic symptoms. Core symptoms — persistent low mood, anhedonia, decreased energy for ≥2 weeks — plus additional symptoms (poor concentration, guilt, pessimism, suicidal ideation, sleep/appetite disturbance) meeting ICD-10 criteria for a severe depressive episode.

What are the differentials?›
  • Adjustment disorder with depressed mood (clear psychosocial stressors — domestic violence, alcoholic husband)
  • Grief reaction, if a recent bereavement exists
  • Depression secondary to anaemia / physical illness
  • Alcohol-related mood disturbance in the family system (not the patient's own use)
What is the relationship between anaemia and depression, and why is it relevant here?›

Anaemia causes fatigue, poor concentration, and low energy that can mimic or worsen depressive symptoms, so it must be corrected before residual symptoms are fully attributed to depression. It's also relevant because SSRIs/SNRIs carry a GI bleeding risk that needs extra care in an anaemic patient.

Do you think inpatient management is needed?›
Yes

High suicidal risk (documented self-harm) plus an unsafe home environment justify admission. On the ward: close observation level, restricting means of self-harm, monitoring oral intake, and stabilising physical comorbidities (anaemia, dysuria).

Pharmacological management?›

First-line SSRI — sertraline. Favoured in medically comorbid patients for its relatively low interaction profile and good cardiac safety.

How do you increase the dose?›

Antidepressants take about 2 weeks to show effect, with maximal benefit over 6–12 weeks. Reassess at 2 weeks; if response is inadequate despite tolerability, adherence, and stressor management, increase the dose rather than switching immediately.

What next — for the sleep issue?›

A short-acting benzodiazepine such as clonazepam for sleep, short-term only, mindful of dependence risk.

Patient already on ECT — what next?›

Continue ECT. Rationale: bringing about improvement rapidly given high suicidal risk. ECT acts faster than antidepressants but doesn't replace pharmacotherapy — continue sertraline alongside.

Non-pharmacological management?›
  • Psychoeducation of patient and family
  • CBT, supportive psychotherapy, interpersonal therapy
  • Behavioural activation
  • Medical social worker involvement for domestic violence / children's welfare
Any referral needed?›

Surgical referral for ongoing dysuria — needs proper work-up, not assumed functional.

Where would you refer for domestic violence / family issues?›

Mithuru Piyasa — the police-linked women & children's desk service that supports domestic violence and child protection cases in Sri Lanka.

ICD-10 criteria for depression?›

Core symptoms (≥2 for 2 weeks): persistent low mood, loss of interest/pleasure, decreased energy/increased fatigability.

Additional symptoms: reduced concentration, reduced self-esteem, ideas of guilt/worthlessness, pessimistic view of the future, ideas of self-harm/suicide, disturbed sleep, diminished appetite.

Severe episode = most/all core symptoms + several additional symptoms, marked distress, functional impairment, with or without psychotic features.

Regarding risk to children — public or government schooling? Who do you collaborate with?›

Class teachers, CPN (community psychiatric nurse), and PSW (psychiatric social worker) — collaborating across school and health systems to monitor the children's welfare.

If you met the husband, what would you do?›

Keep a safe distance and prioritise the patient's safety; advise/refer him to counselling if infidelity or ongoing violence is confirmed, in liaison with social services.

How to differentiate normal grief from abnormal grief in this patient?›

Normal grief: mood fluctuates and gradually improves, guilt focused specifically on the deceased, function largely preserved. Abnormal grief / depressive episode: pervasive unremitting low mood, generalised guilt (not just about the deceased), marked biological symptoms, low self-esteem (not typical of normal grief), duration beyond expected cultural norms, suicidal ideation.

Is admission warranted? Apart from high suicidal risk, what other reasons justify admission?›
  • Risk to children / others in the household
  • Inability to self-care (poor oral intake, self-neglect)
  • Lack of safe social support at home
  • Need for close monitoring during medication titration / ECT
  • Diagnostic clarification
How would you manage her psychosocial problems?›

MDT approach — CBT, behavioural activation, supportive psychotherapy/counselling, grief counselling, legal advice via WIN (Women In Need) given her separated-not-divorced status, PSW involvement (son's substance misuse, children's welfare), OT functional assessment.

Side effects of venlafaxine?›
  • GI: nausea, dyspepsia, constipation
  • Dose-related hypertension; QT changes
  • Hyponatraemia (SIADH)
  • Tachycardia
  • More cardiotoxic in overdose than SSRIs — caution given suicidality
How to manage GORD-like symptoms from venlafaxine (pharmacological and non-pharmacological)?›

Non-pharmacological: smaller, more frequent meals; take with food; avoid known aggravants.
Pharmacological: a PPI (e.g. omeprazole); switch to an SSRI if severe/persistent.

How long do you continue medications?›

At least 6 months after remission of a first episode; up to 2 years or longer for patients at high relapse risk (recurrent episodes, poor support, residual symptoms).

What is the prognosis?›
Good

If first episode, patients can remain recurrence-free for many years with good social support, treatment adherence, and resolution of psychosocial stressors — though ongoing marital conflict here is a relapse risk.

Neurology referral for migraine-like symptoms?›

Accepted — appropriate work-up and management of the headache/migraine picture independent of the psychiatric presentation.

If the patient says she still has suicidal ideas a week later, what would you do?›

Continue close monitoring and reassess psychosocial context; consider increasing the antidepressant dose (it's still early — full response takes ≥2 weeks) while maintaining safety measures, not increasing blindly without reviewing risk holistically.

If abdominal cramps and weakness develop, what do you consider?›
SIADH secondary to venlafaxine

Check serum electrolytes urgently. If Na <125mmol/L: stop the drug, admit, manage as hyponatraemia. Also consider nutritional deficiency, especially with poor intake.

What would the PSW social report cover?›

The husband's behaviour pattern and the children's welfare and safety aspects.

How do you address the husband's alcohol use in a family meeting?›

Motivational interviewing — explore his stage of readiness to change rather than confront; refer to addiction counselling / Mithuru Piyasa if violence is ongoing.

Why might she relapse?›

Ongoing marital conflict and unresolved domestic violence are major relapse risks — address with couple therapy (if safe) and continued social work involvement.

CASE 02

Wasana Kumari, 42–45F

Mania (Bipolar Affective Disorder)
Background: Presented following an aggressive episode with overtalkativeness, grandiose delusions, reduced need for sleep. History of Modecate, Olanzapine, and Lithium at various points.
Diagnosis? Management overview?›

Bipolar affective disorder, current episode mania (with psychotic features if grandiosity is delusional).

Management sequence:

  • Stop any antidepressant
  • Start an antipsychotic — olanzapine, quetiapine, risperidone, or a modest dose of haloperidol (atypicals now preferred first-line)
  • Add a benzodiazepine (lorazepam/clonazepam) adjunctively for agitation/sleep
  • Add/optimise a mood stabiliser if response is unsatisfactory — lithium for classical euphoric mania, valproate for mixed/dysphoric features or if lithium contraindicated
Side effects of sodium valproate?›
  • GI upset, tremor, sedation
  • Weight gain, transient hair loss (texture change on regrowth)
  • Raised liver transaminases; rare hepatotoxicity
  • Thrombocytopenia; rare pancreatitis; hyperammonaemia
  • Teratogenic — avoid in women of childbearing age unless no alternative
Why is the patient on a cannula? Why ECT?›

Possible treatment-resistant mania. ECT is effective in severe mania unresponsive to combination drug treatment — historical response rates around 80% in refractory cases.

Side effects of ECT?›
  • Immediate: headache, muscle ache, nausea, transient confusion
  • Cognitive: anterograde & retrograde memory impairment (usually resolves within weeks)
  • Rare: cardiac arrhythmia during procedure, prolonged seizure, aspiration risk under anaesthesia
How do you prevent relapse?›

Continue mood stabiliser maintenance (≥2 years after a severe episode with significant impact); psychoeducation of patient and family on early relapse signs; lifestyle regularity — sleep hygiene, avoiding substance use.

Restarting olanzapine after stopping an antidepressant — how long to act?›

Sedative/behavioural effects within days; full antimanic effect over 1–2 weeks.

She was on Lithium — check serum levels. How long does lithium take to act? How do you advise on blood checks?›

Sample taken 12 hours post-dose. Therapeutic range: acute mania 0.8–1.2 mmol/L; maintenance 0.6–0.8 mmol/L (as low as 0.4 in some patients). Full prophylactic benefit may take months to establish.

How to prevent relapses / ensure compliance?›

Psychoeducation and family involvement — a supervising family member, regular follow-up, and addressing side-effect burden that undermines adherence.

What was she on for positive symptoms? How much can you increase the dose?›

Oral Risperidone 2mg. Usual range 2–6mg; technically titratable to 16mg, but practically limited to ~4–6mg since EPSE risk rises steeply beyond that, and the drug starts behaving more like a typical antipsychotic.

What are the EPSEs? Features of akathisia?›

EPSEs: acute dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia.

Akathisia: unpleasant inner restlessness, inability to sit still, urge to pace — can be mistaken for worsening psychotic agitation (risking inappropriate dose escalation). Best managed by reducing the antipsychotic dose; propranolol or short-term benzodiazepines help. Anticholinergics are not reliably effective for akathisia specifically.

How do you manage the depressive episode?›

Oral sertraline — discuss GI side effects, sexual dysfunction, hyponatraemia risk.

Why is it given in the morning?›

Can cause activation/insomnia in some patients (though sedation occurs in others) — dosing time is individualised to the patient's response.

Management options for GORD symptoms?›

PPI, dietary modification, dosing with food — as detailed under Case 1.

Why did she relapse?›

Poor compliance, often driven by impaired insight or distressing psychotic symptoms that made consistent engagement difficult — despite seeking help for physical complaints.

Prognosis?›

Variable — good compliance and family psychoeducation minimise relapses; poor compliance and comorbid psychotic features predict a less favourable course.

Psychological management for negative symptoms?›

Rehabilitation via occupational therapy — structured activity, social skills training, vocational rehabilitation — since medication has limited efficacy for negative/cognitive symptoms.

How do you manage the patient on initial presentation to the ward, if acutely agitated? Then once calm?›

On acute presentation: aggressive patient management — verbal de-escalation first, rapid tranquilization (oral if cooperative, IM haloperidol + promethazine or IM olanzapine if not) if there's genuine risk of harm.
Once calm: shift to standard management goals — safe ward environment, collateral history and past records review, then pharmacological optimisation.

Morbid jealousy vs mania — how do you tell the difference when both could explain the presentation?›

This is a genuinely tricky distinction worth naming explicitly if the history includes both an affair/infidelity theme and manic features. Morbid jealousy is a fixed, often delusional belief in a partner's infidelity, typically accompanied by checking behaviours (checking phones, following the partner, demanding evidence) and can occur independent of mood state. Mania can independently generate grandiose or paranoid-tinged ideas about a partner, but these tend to resolve as the mood episode resolves and lack the specific evidence-checking behaviour pattern. If checking behaviours and a fixed conviction are present and don't track with the mood state, morbid jealousy should be considered and explored on collateral history rather than assumed to simply be "part of the mania."

Precipitating factors to explore for the current manic episode?›

Sleep deprivation (e.g. from stress of event planning or family commitments), psychosocial stressors (marital conflict/infidelity concerns), any alcohol intake (even small amounts can destabilise mood in bipolar disorder), head injury or other physical precipitants, and non-adherence to maintenance medication.

Risk to a young child in the household — how do you manage it?›

Involve the PSW to conduct a home visit and provide a social investigation report covering the neighbourhood, home environment, and caregiving capacity; if any concerns emerge, escalate to the Child Protection Authority and the Child and Women's Bureau (police) for further safeguarding action.

Why give ECT for this patient?›

Indicated where there is high suicidal risk, severe/life-threatening physical exhaustion from prolonged mania (dehydration, exhaustion, inability to rest or eat adequately), or treatment resistance to medication — ECT acts faster than pharmacotherapy, which matters when the physical state itself becomes dangerous.

What is the drug in "Rapitab"?›

Rapitab (as used in local rapid tranquilization practice) typically refers to a sublingual/orally-dissolving formulation — most commonly lorazepam — used for rapid calming in agitated patients who can still cooperate with oral treatment, avoiding the need for an immediate injection.

How do you address future pregnancy planning with a patient on lithium?›

Discuss contraception proactively, since lithium is associated with fetal cardiac abnormalities (Ebstein's anomaly) particularly with first-trimester exposure — pregnancy should ideally be planned rather than unplanned so that medication can be reviewed in advance, weighing the risk of relapse if lithium is stopped against the (low absolute) fetal risk if continued.

What is the risk of Ebstein's anomaly with lithium?›

First-trimester lithium exposure increases the relative risk of Ebstein's anomaly by roughly tenfold compared with the general population, but the absolute risk remains low — in the region of 0.05–0.1% (some sources cite ~0.056% in lithium-treated patients vs ~0.023% in controls). Because stopping lithium carries a high risk of relapse, the decision to continue or stop in pregnancy is individualised, weighing relapse risk against this small absolute fetal risk.

Can lithium be given to a breastfeeding woman?›
Generally avoided

Lithium passes freely into breast milk, and infant serum concentrations can approach maternal levels — so breastfeeding is usually best avoided while on lithium, or bottle-feeding advised, given the risk of infant toxicity ("floppy baby"-type effects, cyanosis, hypotonicity).

How do you know the lithium dose is adequate? Serum lithium levels in acute mania vs maintenance?›

Confirm adequacy via serum lithium level, sampled 12 hours post-dose, alongside clinical response.
Acute mania target: 0.8–1.2 mmol/L.
Maintenance target: 0.6–0.8 mmol/L (as low as 0.4 mmol/L in some patients who tolerate/respond at lower levels).

Why choose sodium valproate this time — advantages over lithium?›

Faster onset of action than lithium (useful in acute mania), requires somewhat less intensive monitoring, and has a wider therapeutic index than lithium — making it a practical first choice in the acute phase, particularly if lithium's narrow therapeutic index and monitoring burden are a concern (e.g. in a patient who may not reliably attend for blood tests).

Pre-treatment investigations before starting sodium valproate? Why check FBC specifically?›

FBC and liver function tests before starting — FBC specifically because of the risk of thrombocytopenia/pancytopenia as a side effect of sodium valproate; LFTs because of the (rare) risk of hepatotoxicity.

What is the issue with starting sodium valproate in a woman of childbearing age? Did you ask about contraception?›

Sodium valproate is teratogenic (associated with neural tube defects and other fetal abnormalities), so current guidance is that it should not be prescribed to women of childbearing potential unless no other effective/tolerated alternative exists — and if it must be used, the patient should be established on a reliable method of contraception first, with this discussed and documented explicitly rather than assumed.

What antipsychotic would you start, and why (considering metabolic risk)?›

Ideally aripiprazole given its comparatively favourable metabolic profile — relevant in a patient who already has hypertension or other metabolic risk factors — but if unavailable in the government sector, risperidone is a reasonable practical alternative, chosen over olanzapine specifically because of its relatively lower metabolic/weight-gain side-effect burden.

What to check at follow-up visits on olanzapine? Reasons for relapse? How does sleep affect relapse?›

At follow-up: weight, fasting blood sugar, and lipid profile — olanzapine carries a significant metabolic risk (weight gain, dyslipidaemia, diabetes), so these need proactive monitoring, not just weight alone.

Reasons for relapse: missed sleep/disrupted sleep-wake routine, non-adherence, substance use (including even modest alcohol intake), and ongoing psychosocial stress.

Sleep and relapse: reduced sleep is both an early warning sign and a potential trigger of manic relapse in bipolar disorder — advise the patient on maintaining consistent sleep hygiene and treat any emerging sleep disruption promptly as a relapse-prevention measure, since a vicious cycle can develop where reduced sleep worsens mood instability which further disrupts sleep.

Non-pharmacological management of BPAD overall?›

Psychoeducation of patient and family about the illness, its relapsing course, and early warning signs; relapse-signature planning; family therapy/psychoeducation given the recurring theme of domestic and marital stressors; CBT targeting relapse-related cognitions and behaviours; sleep hygiene; and social support via PSW/CPN.

CASE 03

Sirima Gunawardena

Treatment-Resistant Schizophrenia comorbid with Severe Depression
Background: Transferred with 1-year history of generalized abdominal pain, worsening recently. Nihilistic and hypochondriacal delusions, 2nd/3rd person auditory hallucinations, olfactory and tactile hallucinations, severe depressive symptoms. History of epilepsy.
Definition of nihilistic delusion and hypochondriacal delusion — how do these relate to the patient?›

Nihilistic delusion: a fixed belief that oneself, others, or the world does not exist, or that one's organs are rotting/absent — classically seen in severe psychotic depression (Cotard's syndrome in its extreme form).

Hypochondriacal delusion: a fixed, false belief of having a serious illness despite no medical evidence — fitting her generalized abdominal pain with no organic cause found.

Differentials and working diagnosis?›
Treatment-resistant schizophrenia comorbid with severe depression

Supported by chronicity, multiple hallucination modalities (auditory, olfactory, tactile), mood-incongruent delusions, and prior inadequate response to ≥2 antipsychotics at adequate dose/duration despite compliance — the formal definition of treatment resistance.

Are you happy with the current medication? Main concern starting venlafaxine here?›

Main concerns are cardiovascular (BP elevation, QT changes) and serotonin syndrome risk if combined with other serotonergic drugs; also risk of exacerbating psychotic symptoms if given alone in psychotic depression — usually combined with an antipsychotic rather than given as monotherapy.

What is the optimum (target) dose of clozapine?›

Start 12.5mg at night, titrate slowly (12.5–25mg increments, up to 50–100mg/week increases). Most respond at 300–450mg/day; maximum 900mg/day.

What is the relationship between clozapine and epilepsy?›

Clozapine lowers seizure threshold dose-dependently; seizure risk rises significantly above a plasma level of ~600 µg/L. In a patient with known epilepsy: slower titration, lower target dose where possible, and add sodium valproate as prophylaxis if higher doses are needed — in liaison with the treating neurologist/physician.

How do you improve compliance?›

Family supervision of medication, psychoeducation, addressing side-effect burden, simplifying the regimen.

Detailed psychological management of depression?›

CBT for depressive cognitions, behavioural activation, supportive psychotherapy, family psychoeducation to reduce expressed emotion.

Detailed psychological management of resistant schizophrenia?›

Address high expressed emotion in the family (critical/over-involved communication worsens relapse risk) via family intervention; OT-led rehabilitation for negative/cognitive symptoms given weak pharmacological evidence there; social skills training; vocational rehabilitation.

Can you consider ECT for this patient?›
Yes

ECT can be considered in schizophrenia with poor medication response, especially with prominent affective/catatonic features, and is particularly useful here given the high suicidal risk from her severe depressive component.

Main concern with severe depression / reason for severe depression in this patient?›

The chronic, distressing psychotic symptoms themselves — especially command/critical hallucinations and nihilistic/hypochondriacal delusions — generate severe demoralisation and depression; also the burden of chronic illness and possible post-psychotic depression.

CASE 04

MSDSP Weerasinghe

Schizophrenia / Cannabis-induced Psychosis
Background: History of cannabis use and epilepsy, poor compliance but good family support. On Risperidone, later switched to depot.
Differential diagnosis?›

Schizophrenia vs cannabis-induced psychotic disorder — differentiated by whether psychotic symptoms persist beyond the expected intoxication/withdrawal window once cannabis stops, and whether there's a background of progressive functional decline and negative symptoms independent of substance use.

Treatment setting? Role of KUO nursing officer?›

Given poor compliance and current relapse: inpatient stabilisation initially, transitioning to community follow-up with depot and CPN support. The KUO (out-of-clinic follow-up nursing officer) role includes home visits, monitoring compliance, and liaising between patient/family and the psychiatric team.

Acute management?›

Rapid tranquilization if agitated (oral first-line, then IM if needed), start/restart an oral antipsychotic, and coordinate epilepsy management with the medical team.

Side effects of Risperidone?›
  • EPSE (dose-dependent)
  • Hyperprolactinaemia — galactorrhoea, menstrual disturbance, sexual dysfunction
  • Weight gain, sedation, postural hypotension
Given poor compliance but good family support — what would you do?›
Depot antipsychotic

Switch to a depot with family/CPN supervision of appointments.

What depot antipsychotics are available? Typical or atypical? Doses?›

Typical: fluphenazine decanoate, flupenthixol decanoate, zuclopenthixol decanoate, haloperidol decanoate.
Atypical: risperidone long-acting injection, olanzapine pamoate, paliperidone palmitate, aripiprazole depot.

Typical clinical doses: fluphenazine decanoate ~50mg / 2–5 weeks; flupenthixol decanoate ~60mg / 2–4 weeks.

Regarding substance use, what are you going to do?›

Brief intervention — psychoeducation on harms, motivational interviewing to assess and enhance readiness to change, referral to a specialist drug rehabilitation unit if needed.

What risks did you identify? How to manage overall?›

Recurrent relapse/admission risk due to poor compliance — managed with an IM depot arranged with CPN support (specifically Fluphenazine decanoate).

Where is Fluphenazine decanoate given?›

Deep intramuscular injection, gluteal or deltoid muscle. A test dose is given first to check for hypersensitivity/EPSE, with the full dose following about 7 days later, then continuing on the regular 2–4 weekly schedule.

As a doctor, do you believe the self-reported 1-year abstinence?›
No

Self-report alone is unreliable, especially with ongoing relapse. Verify via urine drug screening.

What stage of the motivational cycle is he in?›

Likely contemplation or even pre-contemplation, given the mismatch between claimed abstinence and clinical picture — explore this directly using motivational interviewing rather than assuming.

Anything for a better outcome?›

Family psychoeducation; appoint a family member as a "treatment supporter" to supervise compliance and administration; liaise with the medical/neurology team for epilepsy control (poor seizure control and substance use interact).

Can he marry?›
Yes, but not immediately

Ideally once he's had a period of sustained stability, good compliance, and insight — with full disclosure to his partner and family involvement in planning.

CASE 05

Hemamalee, 46F

Severe Depression without psychotic symptoms, associated with normal grief
Background: 1 month of headache and sleeplessness. Low mood, low energy, anhedonia. Father died 1 month prior. Family history of suicide (brother, by hanging). Two children with their own difficulties. Husband left her when younger child was 3 months old; similar episode in 2013 treated for 6 months. Active suicidal thoughts controlled by a promise to her late father to protect her mother. Neck lump found incidentally; migraine-like symptoms ~1 year.
Is she in grief — which stage of the grief cycle?›

Grief models (e.g. Kübler-Ross — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance) are a useful framework, but grief doesn't move linearly. I'd describe her current stage descriptively rather than force her into a rigid staged model.

How to differentiate normal grief from abnormal grief in this patient?›

Favouring normal grief: recent bereavement (1 month), some preserved function, suicidal thoughts actively controlled by a meaningful commitment.
Cautionary features: similar past episode in 2013 treated for 6 months (suggests possible recurrent depressive disorder), family history of suicide, active suicidal ideation. On balance this was accepted as normal grief — but with vigilance given the risk factors.

How to manage the patient — pharmacological and psychosocial?›

Pharmacological: an SSRI or SNRI (venlafaxine, starting 37.5mg, titrating to 75mg over about a week given severity).
Psychosocial: psychoeducation, behavioural activation (activity scheduling), grief counselling, supportive psychotherapy, CBT.

How would you manage the problems of each child?›

Son (tobacco use): brief intervention / substance use counselling appropriate to his age.
Daughter (anger issues): anger management, possibly family therapy — conduct disorder considered less likely given her age without other antisocial features.

Is admission warranted? Apart from suicidal risk, what other reasons justify admission?›
Yes

Active suicidal ideation (even if currently controlled) plus a family history of completed suicide. Other reasons: no adult supervision at home during high-risk periods, need for close monitoring during medication initiation, diagnostic clarification of the neck lump.

Medication started? Side effects of venlafaxine? GORD management? Duration? Prognosis?›

As detailed under Case 1 — venlafaxine (dyspepsia, hypertension, hyponatraemia), PPI + dietary measures for GORD, continue ≥6 months (up to 2 years if high relapse risk), prognosis generally good with protective factors present.

How do you prepare a patient for ECT? Post-ECT side effects?›

Pre-ECT: physical examination, ECG, anaesthetic review, informed consent, fasting instructions, minimising benzodiazepine dose beforehand where feasible (benzodiazepines raise seizure threshold and can blunt ECT efficacy).

Post-ECT: immediate confusion, headache, muscle ache, nausea; anterograde/retrograde memory impairment (usually transient).

Other aspects of management?›

Supportive psychotherapy; referral to Mithuru Piyasa given the husband's alcoholism/abandonment history.

Migraine-like symptoms — neurology referral?›

Accepted — the incidental neck lump also needs proper physical work-up (thyroid/surgical/ENT as appropriate), not overlooked amid the psychiatric presentation.

Children's psychological status / future risk?›

Conduct disorder considered less likely given their ages without a persistent antisocial pattern. Future depression risk in the children is difficult to predict definitively given the family history of suicide and parental depression as risk factors — requires ongoing monitoring rather than a firm prediction.

CASE 06

Bandara, 38–39M

Schizophrenia comorbid with Severe Depression / BPAD mania (multiple presentations)
Background: Known psychiatric patient for years, poor compliance, multiple past admissions, ECT twice monthly historically. Extramarital relationship of wife with his brother; aggressive thoughts/threats toward wife and brother; domestic violence. On Imipramine, Venlafaxine, Lithium (stopped). Reported elated mood, increased libido, reduced sleep, overspending. Recently self-harmed with a knife against wife.
Differential diagnosis?›

BPAD current episode mania with psychotic symptoms vs schizophrenia vs schizoaffective disorder vs agitated depression secondary to reactive distress. The formulation genuinely shifts depending on which symptom cluster is emphasised — the key skill is justifying whichever formulation fits the mental state elicited.

What supports a manic episode diagnosis?›

Elevated mood, increased libido, decreased need for sleep, overspending (buying gold), disinhibited behaviour — in the absence of primary negative symptoms or a clear chronic deteriorating course.

Any physical exam findings?›

None found on checking for self-inflicted cuts; poor oral hygiene noted from betel chewing.

Do you want to give a definitive diagnosis?›
Agitated depression

Secondary to distress from his wife's extramarital affair — reflecting a reconsideration after re-eliciting the mood as more agitated than euphoric during history-taking.

Initial management if acutely agitated?›

Verbal de-escalation first. If that fails: rapid tranquilization — oral option first if cooperative, IM if not. Combination of haloperidol + promethazine is more effective than haloperidol alone. IM olanzapine is an alternative. Avoid IM chlorpromazine (muscle pain, abscess, orthostatic hypotension). Midazolam only where resuscitation facilities are available (respiratory depression risk).

What would you monitor?›

EPSE — acute dystonia, akathisia, and NMS (rigidity, hyperthermia, autonomic instability, raised CK). Vital signs (BP, pulse, respiratory rate) every 15 minutes for the first hour post-IM medication.

Do you want cardiac monitoring?›
Yes

Given haloperidol's QT-prolongation risk.

How do you manage acute dystonia?›

IM anticholinergic (e.g. benztropine/procyclidine) — resolves quickly.

Which delusions occur in depression?›

Mood-congruent delusions — nihilistic, guilt-related, hypochondriacal, poverty delusions.

Schizoaffective framing — risk assessment level?›
High

If the patient presents with very aggressive behaviour, how are you going to manage?›

Two-part answer: initial (immediate) management — ensure safety of patient/staff/other patients, remove potential weapons, verbal de-escalation first; and aggressive patient management more broadly — assess for reversible causes (intoxication, pain, akathisia mistaken for agitation), offer oral medication first if the patient is still cooperative, escalate to IM rapid tranquilization only if verbal de-escalation fails and there is genuine risk of harm.

How do you do verbal de-escalation?›

Approach calmly, at eye level (not looming), maintain a safe exit route for both patient and staff, use a low non-confrontational tone, introduce yourself and explain what's happening, actively listen and acknowledge the patient's distress without arguing about delusional content, offer choices (e.g. oral medication rather than injection) to restore a sense of control, and keep instructions simple and non-threatening. Ensure adequate staff presence nearby without crowding the patient.

Side effects of haloperidol? Dose?›

Dose (rapid tranquilization): IM haloperidol 5–10mg, can repeat 4–8 hourly, maximum 18mg/24 hours; best combined with promethazine rather than given alone.

Side effects: extrapyramidal symptoms (acute dystonia, akathisia, parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia — haloperidol has high EPSE liability as a high-potency FGA), QTc prolongation and risk of arrhythmia/sudden death, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, anticholinergic effects, hyperprolactinaemia, postural hypotension, sedation.

Why promethazine? Mechanism of action?›

Promethazine is added because the combination of haloperidol + promethazine is more effective for rapid tranquilization than haloperidol alone, and promethazine's sedative action allows a lower effective haloperidol dose — reducing the EPSE burden (particularly acute dystonia) that haloperidol alone would carry.

Mechanism: promethazine is a first-generation antihistamine (H1 receptor antagonist) with additional anticholinergic and antiadrenergic (alpha-1 blocking) properties, which is what produces its marked sedative effect.

Pharmacokinetics of promethazine?›

Well absorbed both orally and by IM injection; lipophilic, crosses the blood-brain barrier readily (accounting for its central sedative effect); extensively metabolised in the liver; has a relatively long duration of sedative action (single dose sedation typically lasting several hours), making it practical for one-off use in acute tranquilization rather than needing frequent redosing.

How to do physical restraint?›

Physical restraint is a last resort, used only when verbal de-escalation has failed and there's imminent risk of harm. It should be carried out by an adequately sized trained team (typically 4–5 members, one per limb plus one for the head/airway), following a clear pre-agreed plan, minimising force to the least required, protecting the airway and monitoring breathing throughout, avoiding prone restraint or pressure on the chest/neck (asphyxiation risk), keeping restraint time as short as possible, and using it as a bridge to rapid tranquilization rather than a standalone intervention — with full documentation afterwards.

How to identify acute dystonia?›

Occurs within the first few hours of starting/increasing an antipsychotic (antipsychotic-naïve young males at highest risk) — look for spasm of head and neck muscles: torticollis, protrusion of the tongue, spasms of the ocular muscles (oculogyric crisis), trismus. It's frightening for the patient and can look dramatic, but is not itself life-threatening (unlike laryngeal dystonia, which is an emergency due to airway compromise).

Management of dystonia?›

IM anticholinergic — benztropine (or procyclidine) — gives rapid relief, usually within 5–10 minutes. Reassure the patient throughout, as the experience is distressing. If recurrent, consider reducing the antipsychotic dose or adding regular prophylactic anticholinergic cover.

How many times can we give promethazine and haloperidol?›

Haloperidol: repeat 4–8 hourly, up to a maximum of 18mg in 24 hours. Promethazine is generally combined once per haloperidol dose (25–50mg) rather than repeatedly stacked, given its long duration of action — redosing frequency and total daily limits should follow the ward's rapid tranquilization protocol, with vital signs (sedation level, temperature, pulse, BP, respiratory rate) monitored every 10–15 minutes in the first hour, then every 30 minutes until the patient is ambulatory.

How to diagnose akathisia?›

Occurs within a few days of starting/increasing an antipsychotic. Ask specifically about an inner sense of restlessness (not just observe pacing) — the patient feels compelled to move the legs, shift weight, or pace, and finds it very difficult to sit or stand still. Important to distinguish from psychotic agitation, since the two can look similar but require opposite management.

Management of akathisia?›

Reduce the antipsychotic dose or switch to a second-generation antipsychotic — this is the primary step. Anticholinergics are not effective for akathisia (unlike for dystonia/parkinsonism). Propranolol or a short-term benzodiazepine can help symptomatically.

Risperidone is a reasonable first choice here: effective for the positive psychotic symptoms, available and affordable in government hospitals (one of the few atypicals routinely stocked), has a well-established dosing range, and — compared with olanzapine — carries a much lower risk of weight gain and metabolic syndrome, which matters given this patient's young age and long expected treatment duration.

Can't we use Olanzapine?›

This patient had sexual dysfunction — how do you address this?›

First clarify the likely mechanism — decreased dopamine transmission, hyperprolactinaemia, anticholinergic effects, and alpha-1 receptor blockade from the antipsychotic can all contribute (decreased libido, poor arousal, erectile/ejaculatory difficulty). Management: reduce the antipsychotic dose if possible, or switch to an agent with lower potential for sexual side effects — aripiprazole, quetiapine, or clozapine — since these cause little or no prolactin elevation. Also provide advice and reassurance, since the problem often causes secondary anxiety and relationship strain by the time it's disclosed.

How do you give sex therapy?›

Sex therapy is a structured psychological/behavioural treatment, typically involving the couple where possible. It begins with detailed assessment of the problem and its context, followed by education and reassurance (often longstanding anxiety has built up around the problem). Core technique: sensate focus — a graded series of exercises where the couple takes turns giving non-genital, then genital, touch with an explicit initial ban on intercourse, to reduce performance anxiety and rebuild intimacy before reintroducing full sexual activity in stages. Delivered over multiple sessions with a trained therapist, alongside addressing any underlying relationship or psychological contributors.

Any drugs to manage the sexual dysfunction?›

Beyond switching/reducing the antipsychotic: phosphodiesterase type 5 (PDE-5) inhibitors (e.g. sildenafil) are the most widely used physical treatment for erectile dysfunction, once organic contributors (diabetes, hormonal causes) are checked and other high-risk drug interactions excluded.

Cannabis-induced Psychosis vs Schizophrenia (with alcohol use)
Background: Panel 1 — examiners Buddika sir and Lakshika madam. Presentation involving both cannabis use and psychotic symptoms, with alcohol use also elicited on history, and prominent negative symptoms raised for discussion.
History and MSE?›

History structured as: presenting complaint → history of presenting illness, with particular attention to the temporal relationship between substance use and psychotic symptoms (which came first, whether symptoms persist during abstinent periods) → substance use history in detail (cannabis — age of onset, quantity, pattern, last use; alcohol — quantity/frequency, features of dependence) → past psychiatric and family history (any family history of schizophrenia strengthens the case for a primary psychotic illness rather than purely substance-induced) → premorbid personality and functioning.

MSE: appearance/behaviour, speech, mood, thought form and content (delusions), perception (hallucinations), cognition, and — importantly here — a deliberate look for negative symptoms (flattened affect, poverty of speech, avolition, social withdrawal) since their presence or absence is central to the differential.

Diagnosis?›

The working differential sits between cannabis-induced psychotic disorder and schizophrenia (with cannabis as a possible precipitant/risk factor rather than sole cause) — the final diagnosis depends on the longitudinal course, particularly whether psychotic symptoms persist beyond the expected intoxication/withdrawal window once cannabis use stops.

How do you differentiate cannabis-induced psychosis from schizophrenia?›

Key discriminators:

  • Temporal relationship — cannabis-induced psychosis typically arises during or shortly after heavy use/intoxication; schizophrenia can present independent of any temporal link to substance use, though cannabis may still have acted as a trigger in a vulnerable individual
  • Course after abstinence — cannabis-induced psychosis usually resolves within days to a few weeks of stopping use; if florid psychotic symptoms persist well beyond this window despite confirmed abstinence, an underlying primary psychotic disorder is more likely
  • Negative symptoms — prominent negative symptoms (flattened affect, avolition, alogia) and a background of gradual functional decline point towards schizophrenia; cannabis-induced psychosis is more often dominated by positive symptoms without this negative symptom / decline pattern
  • Family history — a family history of schizophrenia favours an underlying primary psychotic illness
  • Premorbid functioning — a longer history of social/occupational decline predating heavy cannabis use favours schizophrenia

In practice, the diagnosis often can't be finalised at a single presentation — a period of monitored abstinence with follow-up is usually needed to see whether symptoms fully resolve.

Management?›

Acute: ensure safety, treat any acute agitation with rapid tranquilization if needed, start an oral antipsychotic (e.g. risperidone) for the psychotic symptoms regardless of final attribution, since both differentials need antipsychotic treatment in the acute phase.

Substance use: brief intervention — psychoeducation on the link between cannabis/alcohol and psychosis, motivational interviewing to explore readiness to change, referral to a specialist drug/alcohol rehabilitation service if dependence is confirmed.

Ongoing: monitor symptom course over weeks of confirmed abstinence to help clarify the diagnosis; family psychoeducation; OT/rehabilitation input if negative symptoms or functional decline are present; regular follow-up to reassess whether long-term antipsychotic maintenance (as in schizophrenia) or time-limited treatment (as in a resolving substance-induced psychosis) is appropriate.

How do you manage if compliance is poor?›

Explore the specific reason first (side effects, poor insight, ongoing substance use disrupting routine, cost, stigma). Then: switch to a long-acting depot antipsychotic (e.g. fluphenazine decanoate — test dose first, full dose about a week later, then 2–4 weekly) if oral adherence is the core problem; involve family in supervising medication; psychoeducation for patient and family on relapse risk; address the substance use in parallel, since continued cannabis/alcohol use both worsens psychotic symptoms and undermines treatment adherence; arrange CPN/community follow-up to catch early signs of relapse or disengagement.

Alcohol dependence criteria?›

Per ICD-10, a diagnosis of dependence syndrome requires 3 or more of the following to have been present together at some point in the previous year:

  • A strong desire or sense of compulsion to take alcohol
  • Difficulty controlling drinking — onset, termination, or level of use
  • A physiological withdrawal state when alcohol is reduced/stopped (or drinking to relieve/avoid withdrawal symptoms)
  • Evidence of tolerance — needing increasing amounts to achieve the same effect
  • Progressive neglect of other pleasures/interests because of drinking, and increased time spent obtaining, using, or recovering from alcohol
  • Persisting with drinking despite clear evidence of harmful physical or mental consequences

Note that dependence always implies harmful use, but harmful use can occur without meeting full dependence criteria.

Negative symptoms?›

Classically remembered as the "four As":

  • Alogia — decreased spontaneous speech / poverty of speech
  • Avolition — decreased motivation and initiative
  • Affective flattening — lack of emotional expressivity (distinct from depression)
  • Anhedonia — loss of pleasure in previously enjoyable activities

These are termed "negative" because they represent a loss of normal functioning (as opposed to positive symptoms — delusions and hallucinations — which are an addition to normal experience). Negative symptoms are more characteristic of chronic schizophrenia than of an acute episode or a purely substance-induced psychosis, tend to respond poorly to antipsychotic medication, and are a key target for psychosocial rehabilitation (OT, social skills training) rather than pharmacotherapy alone. Care is needed not to confuse negative symptoms with depressive symptoms or with antipsychotic-induced parkinsonism, both of which can look similar.

Wasantha Kumara, 45M

Alcohol Dependence with Delirium Tremens; past Cannabis use; evaluated for liver failure
Background: Recurring case across multiple panels. 45-year-old male with 3rd person auditory hallucinations, tactile and visual hallucinations, alcohol dependence, past cannabis history. Evaluated for liver failure. On one presentation: disoriented, tremulous, with hallucinations.
History, MSE, risk assessment, differential diagnosis?›

History should establish the pattern and quantity of alcohol use, features of dependence (see below), time since last drink (critical for withdrawal timing), past withdrawal complications (seizures, DTs), and physical health impact (liver disease). MSE would show disorientation, perceptual disturbance (visual/tactile hallucinations — "formication"-type tactile hallucinations are classic), and autonomic signs on examination.

Differentials for a disoriented patient with tremor and hallucinations: delirium tremens, hepatic encephalopathy (given known/suspected liver failure), Wernicke's encephalopathy, alcohol-induced psychotic disorder (perceptions occurring in clear consciousness, unlike DTs), other causes of delirium (infection, electrolyte disturbance).

Disoriented patient with tremors and hallucinations — what is the diagnosis?›
Delirium tremens (DT)

Features: confusion, agitation, visual and tactile hallucinations, fever, tachycardia, and raised blood pressure — typically developing 24–72 hours after stopping alcohol, the period of highest risk. Clinical features and liver function tests help differentiate delirium due to liver failure from delirium tremens, which matters here given his known liver evaluation.

Management of delirium tremens?›

Manage ideally in a medical/ICU setting, in a calm environment with minimal excess light/sound/stimulation, adequate nutrition and hydration, and monitoring of vital signs. A titrating dose regimen of a benzodiazepine is used — the dose is titrated hourly against severity of symptoms to keep the patient sedated and prevent autonomic hyperactivity, continuing until asymptomatic.

Given evidence of liver failure specifically: use lorazepam (or oxazepam) rather than chlordiazepoxide/diazepam — these shorter-acting benzodiazepines are metabolised by glucuronidation, which is not significantly affected by liver impairment, avoiding drug accumulation and over-sedation.

If giving chlordiazepoxide, how do you give it? Doses?›

For DT (in a patient without significant liver impairment): a titrating regimen — chlordiazepoxide 5–40mg every 2–3 hours, titrated to the severity of withdrawal symptoms (higher than the dose used for uncomplicated withdrawal), continued until the patient is asymptomatic.

For uncomplicated withdrawal (tapering regimen, for context): typically 60–120mg/day in divided doses (6–8 hourly), tapered off over 5–7 days — e.g. Day 1: 30mg mane, 30mg noon, 40mg nocte, reducing daily to complete omission by around day 6.

Potential risk with DT?›
Wernicke's encephalopathy

Alongside withdrawal seizures — both are recognised complications of complicated alcohol withdrawal.

Pharmacological management of Wernicke's encephalopathy? Why thiamine? Why vitamin B1 deficiency occurs?›

Thiamine (Vitamin B1) — given parenterally, as intravenous thiamine 400–600mg three times a day for 2 days, followed by 200–300mg/day for 5 days. Since IV administration carries a small anaphylaxis risk, it should only be given where resuscitation facilities are available. Thiamine should always precede glucose administration in an at-risk patient, since glucose can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy in a thiamine-deficient state.

Why B1 deficiency occurs: chronic heavy alcohol use leads to poor dietary intake, reduced GI absorption of thiamine, and impaired hepatic storage/utilisation — alcohol itself also directly impairs thiamine absorption in the gut.

Diagnosis and management overall — how do you address poor compliance? What drugs? Alcohol dependence management overall?›

Poor compliance: explore the reason (ambivalence, ongoing craving, poor insight into dependence, lack of social support), engage family/community support, use motivational interviewing to build readiness for change rather than confrontation.

Long-term management of alcohol dependence: combines psychosocial and pharmacological approaches — psychoeducation, motivational enhancement therapy, relapse prevention (identifying triggers/high-risk situations, urge surfing, refusal skills training), CBT, and self-help groups (Alcoholics Anonymous). Pharmacologically: acamprosate (reduces craving by normalising glutamate/GABA balance, dosed by body weight — e.g. 666mg three times daily if >60kg — continued ≥6 months) or naltrexone (opioid antagonist, blocks the reinforcing/rewarding effects of alcohol, reduces relapse and heavy drinking days) — both are more effective combined with psychological therapy than alone.

What happens if disulfiram is taken with alcohol?›

Disulfiram inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase, so if alcohol is consumed, acetaldehyde accumulates, producing an unpleasant and potentially dangerous reaction: facial flushing, throbbing headache, palpitations, tachycardia, nausea and vomiting — and in vulnerable patients, hypotension, collapse, arrhythmias, and rarely death. It works as a deterrent rather than reducing craving itself, so it suits motivated patients who want additional external support, ideally with supervised administration.

Non-pharmacological management of alcohol dependence — how do you do CBT / manage craving?›

Urge surfing — teaching the patient to let cravings occur, peak, and pass without either fighting or giving in to them, treating the urge as a wave to be "ridden out" rather than resisted or acted upon.
Distraction techniques — engaging in an alternative activity, drinking a non-alcoholic beverage, or discussing the craving with another person until it passes. Both techniques are combined with identifying and planning for high-risk situations and training in substance refusal skills.

  • Temporal lobe: memory, receptive language (Wernicke's area), auditory processing — implicated in some psychotic and mood symptoms, and in temporal lobe epilepsy-related psychiatric presentations
  • Parietal lobe: sensory integration, spatial awareness
  • Occipital lobe: visual processing

Head injury affecting frontal or temporal regions in particular can produce personality change and psychotic-type symptoms, relevant to considering an organic contribution here.

Kept concise to the core steps: exclude/confirm an organic cause (physical examination, neuroimaging, relevant investigations) in parallel with treating the acute psychotic symptoms (antipsychotic medication), ensure safety, involve neurology/medical teams if an organic contribution is confirmed, and psychoeducation/follow-up once stabilised.

Psychotic patients often have impaired insight and an unreliable or incomplete account of their own symptoms and history — collateral history from family fills gaps in timeline (including confirming details of the head injury and its aftermath), verifies substance use and compliance, and helps distinguish between competing differentials (organic vs primary psychotic vs substance-induced) that the patient's own account alone cannot resolve.

Background: 2nd person auditory hallucinations, thought insertion, ± visual hallucinations, high suicidal risk. The depressive component was uncertain on presentation — possibly reflecting prominent negative symptoms rather than a true comorbid depressive episode.
History, MSE, risk assessment?›

Full history and MSE presented without interruption in this panel — a reminder that examiners will sometimes let the candidate run the full structure uninterrupted, so a clear, systematic, well-organised presentation (appearance/behaviour → speech → mood → thought → perception → cognition → insight, then risk) matters even more when there's no prompting.

Risk assessment: high suicidal risk given the presentation — explore intent, plan, access to means, and protective factors explicitly.

Distinguishing negative symptoms from a true comorbid depressive episode here?›

This distinction is genuinely difficult and worth acknowledging as such. Pointers towards negative symptoms rather than depression: flattened affect without the subjective distress/low mood that patients with depression typically report, avolition without pervasive guilt or hopelessness, and a chronic stable pattern rather than an episodic depressive course. Pointers towards true comorbid depression: patient-reported low mood, guilt, hopelessness, and biological symptoms (sleep/appetite change) alongside the psychotic features. The Calgary Depression Scale for Schizophrenia is a validated tool designed specifically to help make this distinction in clinical practice.

Management? How do you manage the suicidal risk?›

Admission given high suicidal risk, close observation, means restriction, treat the underlying psychotic/depressive presentation pharmacologically, and involve the MDT (psychiatrist, CPN, PSW, OT) for a comprehensive plan. If risk remains very high despite antipsychotic/antidepressant treatment, ECT can be considered given its faster onset of action.

Olanzapine maximum dose?›

20mg/day is the standard maximum oral dose for schizophrenia/acute mania.

CASE 21

28M, "chip-controlling-thoughts" delusion (also presented as Udan, 26M)

Schizoaffective Disorder + Polysubstance Use Disorder
Background: Unemployed, chronic mental illness for 5 years, on monthly IM depot antipsychotic. Polysubstance use disorder with dependence present, no current withdrawal symptoms, last use of all substances 2 months ago. Bizarre delusion (believes a chip is controlling his thoughts), thought insertion, thought echo, running commentary auditory hallucinations, grandiose delusion, persecutory delusion, delusion of control.
History, MSE, risk assessment, diagnosis?›
Schizoaffective disorder

Justified by the presence of affective symptoms alongside definite first-rank symptoms of schizophrenia (thought insertion, thought echo, running commentary hallucinations) occurring together — meeting the requirement that both symptom clusters be prominent and concurrent, not just present at different points in the illness course.

How will you confirm the psychotic symptoms are not due to substance use?›
Urine toxicology screening

For cannabis and other substances — noting that cannabis metabolites can remain detectable in urine for about 3–4 weeks in chronic/heavy users, so timing of the test relative to last use needs to be considered when interpreting a negative result.

Management — from collateral history through to considering clozapine?›

Structured as: collateral history (confirm timeline, substance pattern, medication adherence) → relevant investigations (FBC, LFT, RFT, FBS, lipid profile — baseline and metabolic monitoring given long-term antipsychotic use) → acute management (address any acute risk/agitation) → long-term management (optimise current depot, address substance use) → escalation to clozapine if treatment resistance is confirmed (inadequate response to two adequate antipsychotic trials).

Is he able to take clozapine, given he's already on a depot IM antipsychotic?›
No — not straightforwardly

Clozapine requires very close initial monitoring (physical observation for hypotension, weekly FBC initially) and a controlled, gradual oral titration — this doesn't combine cleanly with an existing long-acting depot still active in his system, and starting clozapine typically requires the prior antipsychotic to be tapered/stopped first, with the decision made by a consultant psychiatrist after confirming genuine treatment resistance and adequate adherence to rule out poor compliance as the real reason for relapse.

Reasons for relapse?›
  • Ongoing substance use
  • Poor medication adherence
  • Living alone / poor social support
Non-pharmacological management for the substance use?›

Motivational interviewing — exploring and building his own motivation to change rather than confronting or lecturing.

(Related presentation) Reasons for poor compliance? Modecate dose and form? If poor compliance to Modecate, what next? How to manage cannabis dependence?›

Reasons for poor compliance: poor insight, substance use disrupting routine, side-effect burden, lack of social support, cost/access issues.

Modecate (fluphenazine decanoate): available as an oily IM injection; test dose first (~12.5mg), full dose 4–7 days later, then continued every 2–4 weeks, titrated by response.

If poor compliance persists despite depot: intensify community psychiatric nurse (CPN) follow-up — home visits, directly observed administration, closer monitoring for early relapse signs — and involve family supervision.

Cannabis dependence management: motivational interviewing, psychoeducation on the link between cannabis and relapse/poor treatment response, referral to specialist substance use services if needed.

CASE 22

Harishchandra, 28M (Kalawewa / Mihinthale)

Schizophrenia vs Substance-Induced Psychosis, progressing through treatment resistance to Clozapine
Background: Unemployed, admitted by family/police following aggression. Thought insertion, thought withdrawal (no thought broadcasting or thought echo). 3rd and 2nd person auditory hallucinations for 3 months. Dependence on cannabis and alcohol, last intake of both 2 months ago; also past use of heroin (once), pregabalin, methamphetamine, and other substances in some versions.
History, MSE — any important points? Risk assessment?›

History up to past psychiatric history, with specific attention to any important positive finding elsewhere in the history (e.g. forensic history given police involvement, or specific substance use timeline). MSE as standard. Risk assessment sometimes not explicitly asked in this panel sequence, but should still be considered/mentioned proactively — given aggression severe enough to prompt police involvement, risk to others is a key feature here.

Diagnosis and differentials? How do you differentiate substance-induced psychosis from schizophrenia?›

Differentials: schizophrenia vs schizoaffective disorder vs substance-induced psychosis.

Key differentiator: the psychotic symptoms here preceded the substance misuse in timeline / persisted independent of the pattern of substance use — when the psychotic symptoms clearly predate or continue well beyond the expected window of intoxication/withdrawal, this favours an underlying primary psychotic disorder over a purely substance-induced picture.

How does cannabis affect schizophrenia?›

Cannabis use in patients with schizophrenia is associated with a higher relapse rate and poorer response to treatment — it doesn't just trigger episodes but appears to worsen the overall course and treatment responsiveness of the illness.

Management — treatment setting through to pharmacological management?›

Start from deciding the treatment setting (admission given aggression/police involvement), then collateral history and past records review, then pharmacological management: Risperidone 2mg with side effects (EPSE, hyperprolactinaemia) discussed.

If no response to Risperidone, what next? If no response to that?›

Switch to olanzapine if risperidone at an adequate dose/duration is inadequate. If olanzapine also fails at an adequate trial, this now meets criteria for treatment-resistant schizophrenia (inadequate response to two antipsychotics at adequate dose and duration) — manage with clozapine.

Clozapine — doses, monitoring, side effects to watch for?›

Starting dose: 12.5mg nocte, titrated slowly (12.5–25mg increments, 50–100mg/week increases), most respond at 300–450mg/day, maximum 900mg/day.

Baseline investigations before starting: weight, serum lipids, fasting blood sugar, LFTs, FBC, ECG, echocardiogram. Admit to hospital to start, given risk of hypotension after the initial dose — monitor BP, temperature, and pulse in the first 6 hours.

Key side effects to monitor for: agranulocytosis/neutropenia (weekly FBC for the first 18 weeks, then monthly), paralytic ileus from hypersalivation/constipation-related bowel hypomotility — identified on abdominal exam by abdominal mass and absent bowel sounds — and myocarditis, which can present with fever as an early warning sign and requires urgent cardiac assessment.

CASE 23

Mrs. Dharmaprema, 67F

Severe Depression with Psychotic Symptoms comorbid with Pseudodementia
Background: Widowed, lives alone. Presented with memory loss (~6 months) and low mood (~1 year), with a past suicide attempt (sleeping pills). Believes relatives neglect her and are watching her via CCTV to send her to a mental hospital (persecutory-type delusion) — also described as reference-type ideas about lights near a nearby army camp being about her, and being called a thief. Poor self-care in places, apraxia-like features. History of eye surgery, hypertension, and dyslipidaemia. Poor compliance due to forgetfulness. Homicidal thoughts towards her brother's son. Family history of MI and psychiatric illness. In one version, delusion of reference and visual hallucinations emerged later in the interview.
History, MSE, risk assessment?›

History should explore the timeline relationship between the memory complaints and the mood symptoms — here, the depressive symptoms/low mood (~1 year) predate the memory loss (~6 months), which is an important clue. MSE should specifically probe cognition (attention, concentration, recall) alongside mood and psychotic content (persecutory/referential ideas). Homicidal ideation towards a family member and a past suicide attempt both need explicit, careful risk assessment — this patient carries both self-harm and harm-to-others risk.

What is the diagnosis? What are the differentials?›
Severe depressive episode with psychotic symptoms, comorbid with pseudodementia

Differentials to consider and discuss:

  • Vascular dementia — genuinely worth considering given her age and vascular risk factors (hypertension, dyslipidaemia), but the acute-ish onset of memory complaints and clear preceding depressive symptoms argue against a primary dementia
  • Schizophrenia / schizoaffective disorder — given the persecutory and referential delusions; but the prominent, longstanding low mood and the pattern of memory complaints fitting pseudodementia better support a primary depressive illness with psychotic features
  • True (organic) dementia with secondary depression — the reverse causal direction, which is why the timeline is the deciding factor
How do you differentiate pseudodementia from true dementia here?›

Features favouring pseudodementia (depression causing apparent cognitive impairment): the onset of memory complaints is more acute and closely follows the depressive symptoms rather than preceding them; the patient's own complaints of memory loss are often more prominent/exaggerated than what family/informants actually observe in day-to-day function; she gives many "I don't know" answers on cognitive testing rather than confabulating or making genuine errors; there's a personal history of depression; and cognitive function is expected to normalise once the depressive episode is treated. True dementia, by contrast, shows gradual progressive decline with disorientation typically only in later stages, and impairment is usually consistent between formal testing and everyday functioning as reported by family.

Management — treatment setting, and the suicidal/homicidal risk?›

Admission is warranted, driven specifically by the combination of past suicide attempt, ongoing psychotic symptoms with poor insight, homicidal ideation towards a family member, poor social support (lives alone), and poor medication compliance due to forgetfulness — all of which mean she cannot be safely monitored or managed at home.

Pharmacological management? What if the psychotic symptoms don't respond?›

Sertraline — a reasonable first SSRI choice, reassessing response at around 2 weeks minimum before considering escalation. Risperidone 2mg nocte for the psychotic symptoms, justified specifically because of the severity and psychotic features (not simply added routinely to every depression).

If sertraline alone isn't sufficient for a severe depressive episode: venlafaxine can be considered — but caution is needed here given her hypertension, since venlafaxine causes a dose-related rise in blood pressure; this needs to be monitored if used.

If no response to venlafaxine, and given active suicidal ideas: lithium augmentation is a reasonable option (lithium augmentation has evidence in treatment-resistant depression, and there is evidence it may specifically reduce suicidal risk) — but this needs careful thought given her age and the practical difficulty of close monitoring since she lives alone (risk of toxicity going undetected). If augmentation strategies fail or risk remains very high: ECT, along with proper preparation — consent, fasting, physical/anaesthetic workup.

Non-pharmacological management?›

Occupational therapy input alongside CBT; a PSW social investigation report given the significant social problems (lives alone, family conflict/neglect concerns, poor support network) to inform a realistic discharge and follow-up plan.

CASE 24

Mrs. Saduni Shashikala

Recurrent Depressive Disorder, current episode severe without psychosis, comorbid with OCD and Borderline Personality Disorder traits
Background: Multiple past suicide attempts by varied methods (drug overdose x2, burns, kerosene ingestion). Family conflict and poor medication compliance identified as triggering factors. Occasional substance use (beer, last ~6 months prior). Past history includes a first depressive episode, followed by a period of overactivity/reduced sleep need raising the possibility of a manic episode (though never admitted for it, unclear if ever formally treated as BPAD). Past lithium use, since discontinued. Overweight (BMI raised, ~86kg). Self-harm scars (burns) on examination. OCD symptoms — contamination/dirt obsessions with checking rituals. Insight described as reasonably good.
History, MSE — is this Bipolar Affective Disorder?›

Careful history-taking here needs to establish whether the reported past "overactivity and reduced sleep" truly meets criteria for a manic/hypomanic episode (duration, functional impact, whether it needed treatment) or whether it was a brief, non-diagnostic period of elevated energy. In this case it was judged not to meet criteria for a manic episode — so the working diagnosis stayed as recurrent depressive disorder rather than BPAD, a useful reminder not to over-call bipolarity from a vague history of "feeling more active" without clear diagnostic threshold features.

Premorbid personality history: should explicitly screen for personality disorder traits given the recurrent self-harm — here, borderline personality traits were identified (though the candidate wasn't able to elaborate further on which specific traits when asked — worth being ready to describe: affective instability, impulsivity, unstable relationships, chronic emptiness, recurrent self-harm/suicidal behaviour, fear of abandonment).

Diagnosis and differentials?›
Recurrent depressive disorder, current episode severe without psychosis, comorbid with OCD and borderline personality disorder traits

This is a genuinely complex, multi-axis presentation — the OCD (contamination obsessions with checking compulsions) and the borderline traits both need to be assessed and managed alongside the mood disorder, not treated as incidental findings.

Management — as the OPD MO, and given the suicidal risk, what setting?›

Given the risk profile (multiple past attempts, current severe episode), inpatient management is warranted for safety, initial stabilisation, and close monitoring while starting/adjusting medication.

Pharmacological management — SSRI choice and dose? Side effects?›

Fluoxetine is a sensible choice here specifically because it's effective for both the depressive episode and the comorbid OCD — a practical advantage of treating two conditions with one drug rather than layering separate agents. Alternatively, venlafaxine 75mg mane was also discussed in one version of this case, with its associated side effects (GI upset, dose-related hypertension, hyponatraemia).

If no improvement after 2 weeks: too early to expect full response — continue monitoring closely (e.g. daily review as an inpatient) rather than switching prematurely; a proper adequate trial (usually assessed at 4–6 weeks at an optimised dose) is needed before concluding non-response and changing treatment.

Anything to add given ongoing suicidal risk? Why not lithium augmentation here?›

ECT was the accepted answer given the high suicidal risk needing a fast-acting intervention. Lithium augmentation is a reasonable idea in principle (and does have evidence for reducing suicidal behaviour), but given the past history of prior lithium use being discontinued (reason unclear — possibly due to the suicide risk itself, given lithium's narrow therapeutic index and danger in overdose) and her overall risk profile, it needs more caution here than in a lower-risk patient — worth acknowledging this tension explicitly if asked, rather than reflexively reaching for lithium in every treatment-resistant/high-risk depression.

Non-pharmacological / psychological management? How do you address the domestic violence and its effect on the children?›

Psychotherapy: CBT targeting both the depressive cognitions and the OCD contamination/checking rituals (exposure and response prevention specifically for the latter); given the borderline traits, longer-term consideration of dialectical behaviour therapy (DBT)-informed approaches for emotional regulation and reducing self-harm would also be relevant, though not always explicitly asked for at this level.

Domestic violence and children (13 and 8 years old): involve PSW for a social/child welfare assessment; if the children have been exposed to domestic violence and to a parental psychiatric admission, consider the psychological impact on them directly (fear, disruption of routine, possible modelling of conflict) — arrange age-appropriate explanation/support for the children, liaise with school where relevant, and refer to child protection services (or Mithuru Piyasa-type services) if the domestic violence is ongoing and unaddressed.
