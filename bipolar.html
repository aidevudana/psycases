1Introduction

Mr. H.T. Jayasooriya, age unclear in scan male patient, from Kahatagasdigiliya. History was obtained from the patient and his ~55-year-old mother.

2Presenting Complaint

Patient was taken to the ward by family members due to increased activity, overtalkativeness, decreased need for sleep and irritability, for 15 days duration.

3History of Presenting Complaint

He is a known patient with a psychiatric illness since 2019, but defaulted for 1 year. This is his 3rd admission in THA (Teaching Hospital Anuradhapura).

15 days back, he came home after resigning from his job, where he works in a garment factory. While he was working there, he posted some inappropriate things on social media insulting his relatives. His co-workers also told him he is failing badly, and they called the patient's parents. Parents asked him to come back home, so he came home 15 days ago.

According to the parents, once back home he had overactive behaviour & increased talkativeness. He became irritable with small things, and sometimes shouted at his father. His sleeping time also reduced, as he doesn't feel he needs it. During the last 6 days these symptoms worsened and he started singing alone loudly, which he had not done previously.

Furthermore, despite financial instability, he had been spending unusually on purchasing requisites for the temple. He claimed that he wants to buy land once discharged from hospital. He wants to expand his YouTube channel and make a lot of money.

He also came up with the idea that in 2030 he will become President. He wants to become a teacher through online classes. He also plans to do an English and IT course. Once discharged from hospital, he wants to redo his Advanced Level examination in the Biology stream again, even though he has done his A/Ls in the Arts stream previously, as he now wishes to become a surgeon.

He believes that some of his relatives have forced him to come and be admitted, in relation to a dispute over lands belonging to the patient, and that they brought him here against his will / "illegally".

Because of this, his functional level is impaired: he resigned from his job as he could not work with his co-workers anymore. He complains that during his hospital stay he could not continue his studies (the English & IT course he wants to do).

According to the parents, he had experienced similar symptoms 1 year back. He also had a single previous episode of low mood, reduced social interactions, and lack of energy, which led to poor school attendance as well — this was his first episode.

His drug compliance is very poor, as he doesn't believe he has an illness. After this admission he was given some oral medications and started on Lithium. Even after 3 weeks there has not been much improvement, and he has refused to try ECT.

He believes his thoughts are his own and no external force has inserted or withdrawn them. He denies that his thoughts are known to others without him communicating with them. He denies hearing voices when he is alone.

He has no history of alcohol, tobacco, or any other psychoactive substance misuse. He has not experienced any early morning headache, vomiting, or any recent history of head injury.

Even though he could not do his job, he has good self-care and actively participates in ward activities. He only has moderate functional impairment. He denies any suicidal thoughts, no past history of suicidal attempts or self-harm injuries. No intention to harm anyone.

Manic episode — core features

The Shorter Oxford Textbook characterises mania by elevated/irritable mood plus increased activity or energy, present most of the day for at least a week (or any duration if hospitalisation required), with additional features such as grandiosity, decreased need for sleep, pressured speech, flight of ideas, distractibility, and excessive involvement in risky activities. This history demonstrates classic grandiose delusions (becoming President, becoming a surgeon), overspending, decreased sleep need, and pressured/overtalkative behaviour — the presence of mood-congruent grandiose delusional content is what upgrades this from hypomania/mania to "mania with psychotic symptoms."

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Mood (Affective) Disorders.

6Past Psychiatric History

The first episode of illness was in 2019, where he was frightened following an elephant attack while he was at home with his mother. The patient claims that at that time one of his uncle's spirits had entered his body, and then he became unwell. His mother said the patient did not talk much with anyone after that incident, and he refused to go to school. They then arranged some traditional rituals, and the patient believed his uncle's spirit had left his body. He did not attend hospital at that time. He again developed similar abnormal behaviour some months later, this time given some oral medication; after taking these he did not take them properly.

In between episodes, he was able to return to normal function and had no functional impairment. As mentioned, no history of any suicidal attempts or deliberate self-harm, no history of violence.

Even with his illness, he was able to do day to day activities, and was able to do all his mental state examinations. He only had mild functional impairment at that time. No abnormal weight gain, but complains of recent-onset fine bilateral hand tremors.

7Past Medical / Surgical History

Unremarkable.

8Premorbid Personality

Before the onset of this disease, he was functionally normal, had good relationships with parents, relatives & co-workers, participated in various religious activities, and still likes to do so. According to the parents, he was a very calm person who rarely became irritable. In his leisure time he goes to the temple, sometimes with his parents. He happily helped his parents with housework and cultivation. Whenever he had a problem, he used to tell everything to his parents to ventilate his emotions. When stressed, he likes to meditate, which gives him much relief and comfort.

★ Risk Assessment

To self — Low/Moderate (financial risk). Before admission, he had overspending; he had access to credit cards and bank accounts, and is at risk of selling properties. He has increased libido and sexual disinhibition. No suicidal thoughts, no plans, no previous attempts or self-harm. He has good self-care — hygiene, food intake, and water intake are satisfactory. He dresses in clean, washed clothes, so risk of self-neglect is low.

However, he has ongoing stressors, as he worries he would not be able to continue his studies and get a job. As protective factors, he has good coping skills like meditation, and he is non-alcoholic with no use of psychoactive substances.

So his risk to self (financial/functional) is moderate; his suicidal risk is low.

To others — Low. During relapses he becomes irritable & increasingly talkative, sometimes showing aggression towards his parents. But he only shouts at them, with no history of behavioural problems or violence towards others — usually getting into arguments only. No ideas to harm anyone. So his risk to others is low.

Risk assessment in mania

Unlike depressive episodes where suicide risk dominates, the Shorter Oxford Textbook highlights that the principal risks in mania are often behavioural/financial and reputational (overspending, sexual disinhibition, risky ventures, damaged relationships and employment) rather than suicidal risk, though risk to others through irritability and poor judgement should still be assessed. This case is a textbook illustration — low suicidal risk, but high risk from impaired judgement/overspending, which is precisely why in-ward admission and safeguarding of finances/possessions is recommended.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Risk Assessment and Mania Management.

Flight of ideas vs. formal thought disorder

The Shorter Oxford Textbook distinguishes flight of ideas (rapid, logically-connected shifts between topics, often driven by clang associations or environmental cues, typical of mania) from formal thought disorder seen in schizophrenia (loosening of association, derailment, where the logical connections are lost). Examiners often ask candidates to demonstrate this distinction — this case explicitly documents "a logical connection to his speech" despite the rapid topic changes, supporting flight of ideas rather than schizophrenic thought disorder.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Symptoms and Signs of Mental Disorder.

Mania with psychosis vs. schizoaffective disorder

The key differentiator, per the Shorter Oxford Textbook, is that in bipolar disorder with psychotic features, the psychotic content (here, mood-congruent grandiose delusions) occurs only during the mood episode and resolves as mood normalises, with full inter-episode recovery as described here. Schizoaffective disorder requires psychotic symptoms independent of, and outlasting, the mood episode. Substance-induced mood disorder is excluded by the clear denial of alcohol, tobacco, or psychoactive substance use.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Mood Disorders and Schizoaffective Disorder.

Immediate Management

(1) Decide on treatment setting. I recommend managing him in-ward because: he has poor insight; he has a risk of overspending/financial harm; poor drug compliance.

(2) Provide supportive environment. My priority would be to provide a supportive and structured environment for minimising any potential risk to himself and others.

(3) Assess the patient. I would conduct a thorough assessment including detailed history from him and collateral history from the parents. Then a thorough physical examination and mental state examination. I would go through his past medical records if available, to determine whether he was previously on any antipsychotics, and what they were. Then I would order baseline investigations including FBC, liver function tests, renal function tests, lipid profile, thyroid function test, FBS & serum electrolytes (because if the patient has some underlying disease, side effects of the drugs could exacerbate it).

(4) Educating the patient and family. BAD is a chronic illness that can cause recurrent episodes. I would educate the patient and family members about the disease, aetiologies, symptoms, treatment options and adverse effects of those treatments — and why this patient needs admission and what the management plan is.

(5) Pharmacological management. I would go through the patient's medical records if available and check which medications he was previously on, then assess his response to those drugs. If good response, I would continue those drugs at the same dose or with adjustment.

As this patient has defaulted clinic follow-up, I would start him on an antipsychotic along with a mood stabiliser.

  • Atypical antipsychotic with sedative action — Risperidone 2mg nocte, or Olanzapine 5–10mg nocte, or Quetiapine 100–200mg nocte — and monitor response over adequate time.
  • Mood stabiliser — Sodium Valproate, starting 200mg nocte, gradually increasing up to 600–1000mg/day; or Lithium, starting 400mg nocte, increasing up to 900mg/day.
  • If sleep disturbance only — short course of Clonazepam 0.5mg nocte.
  • If needed, oral Propranolol as an adjunct agent for tremors.

I would also monitor hydration/nutrition status, and monitor current symptoms/compliance/side effects/behaviour/sleep/appetite/self-care/violence carefully with the help of nurses.

If the patient exhibits agitation or aggression, I would first attempt verbal de-escalation. If that fails, I would offer oral Lorazepam 1–2mg with his consent. If that is also not possible, I would consider physical restraint & rapid tranquilisation.

Pharmacological principles in acute mania

The Shorter Oxford Textbook recommends antipsychotics (often with sedative properties) as first-line for rapid control of acute manic symptoms, combined with or followed by introduction of a mood stabiliser (lithium or valproate) for longer-term control, since mood stabilisers alone have a slower onset of action. Benzodiazepines are used adjunctively, short-term, for agitation/insomnia, and should be tapered rather than continued long-term. This plan follows that sequence closely.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Mood Stabilisers and Management of Mania.

Intermediate Management

I would conduct daily ward rounds to assess symptoms, side effects, risk assessment, and medication adherence. If response is poor, I would consider increasing the dose; if still poor, I would consider combination treatment or cross-tapering to another drug. I would consider ECT if response remains poor despite adequate doses of treatment.

I would regularly monitor BP/PR/saturation/weight. Meanwhile, I would arrange a family meeting to obtain collateral history, educate about the disease, identify the aetiology, and reinforce the importance of compliance.

I would encourage the patient to engage in ward activities. If there is functional impairment, I would refer him to rehabilitation and an occupational therapist.

I would slowly taper off the benzodiazepine before discharge. As symptoms improve, I would arrange a parole leave for a few days to a week, and on return assess improvement & side effects of drugs, considering any dose adjustments.

Long-term Management

My long-term management goals are to prepare a discharge plan and arrange follow-up care. Once acute symptoms have improved, I would prepare a discharge plan including a summary of clinical features, treatments, follow-up plan and key contact person. On the discharge card, I would refer him to the closest hospital with psychiatric services, and inform the clinic and community psychiatric nurse about this patient.

I would educate him about strict compliance with the mood stabiliser (maintenance), and discuss the consequences of stopping it abruptly. I would assess drug compliance at follow-up visits, and educate about early warning signs of relapse, and financial and social issues.

I would also assess for any side effects of drugs, social and family functioning, and the level of support available to help him maintain mood stability. I would ask him to identify triggers that cause mood changes.

Long-term relapse prevention in BAD

The Shorter Oxford Textbook emphasises that bipolar disorder is a recurrent, lifelong condition, and long-term maintenance therapy (usually a mood stabiliser ± antipsychotic) is recommended after more than one manic episode, alongside psychoeducation about early warning signs, sleep hygiene, and avoiding triggers (financial stress, substance use, sleep deprivation). Identifying individual relapse signatures (as the plan does here by asking the patient to identify his own triggers) is a core relapse-prevention strategy.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Course and Long-term Management of Bipolar Disorder.

Monitoring lithium and valproate

The Shorter Oxford Textbook stresses regular lithium level monitoring (narrow therapeutic index, risk of toxicity with dehydration/renal impairment/NSAIDs), along with periodic renal and thyroid function tests. Valproate requires monitoring of liver function and full blood count, and is contraindicated/used with extreme caution in women of childbearing potential due to teratogenicity — not directly relevant here (male patient) but a common viva follow-up question.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Mood Stabilisers — Monitoring and Toxicity.

QWhat is your diagnosis, and why does it include "with psychotic symptoms"?

A: Bipolar Affective Disorder, current episode mania with psychotic symptoms. He has the core features of mania — elevated/irritable mood, increased energy, decreased need for sleep, pressure of speech — lasting well over a week, plus mood-congruent grandiose delusions (becoming President, becoming a surgeon), which is what upgrades the episode from simple mania to mania with psychotic symptoms.

QHow do you distinguish his flight of ideas from the formal thought disorder seen in schizophrenia?

A: Flight of ideas is rapid movement between topics that retains a logical or associative connection, which is explicitly documented here. Formal thought disorder (as in schizophrenia) involves loosening of association or derailment, where the logical thread is lost — not present in this history.

QWhy is his suicidal risk graded low, while his financial/functional risk is graded high?

A: Unlike depression, the principal risks in mania are often behavioural and financial rather than suicidal — this is a textbook example. He denies any suicidal ideation or past attempts, but has already resigned from his job, overspent on impulsive purchases, and has stated intentions to buy land and enrol in unrealistic courses — all reflecting impaired judgement rather than risk to life.

QWhat organic and substance causes must be excluded before diagnosing mania, and how were they excluded here?

A: Thyroid disease, corticosteroid use, head injury, and substance-induced mood disorder (amphetamines, cocaine). In this history, he explicitly denies alcohol, tobacco, and any psychoactive substance use, denies head trauma, and there is no history suggestive of steroid use or thyroid symptoms — allowing a primary bipolar diagnosis.

QWhy start both an antipsychotic and a mood stabiliser together, rather than a mood stabiliser alone?

A: Antipsychotics act quickly to control acute manic symptoms, while mood stabilisers such as lithium or valproate have a slower onset of action but are needed for longer-term relapse prevention. Combining them covers both the acute presentation and the maintenance phase from the outset.

QHow would you differentiate this from schizoaffective disorder?

A: In bipolar disorder with psychotic features, the psychotic content occurs only during the mood episode and resolves as the mood normalises, with full recovery between episodes — exactly what is described here. Schizoaffective disorder requires psychotic symptoms that persist independently of, and outlast, the mood episode.

QWhat would you want to check before and during lithium treatment in this patient?

A: Baseline renal function, thyroid function, calcium, and an ECG/pregnancy test if indicated, then lithium levels 12 hours post-dose starting at 7 days, then every 2 weeks until stable, then 3-monthly. His new fine bilateral tremors are also worth noting as a baseline finding to distinguish from a later lithium-induced tremor.

QHe attributed his first episode to a spirit entering his body after an elephant attack. How does this affect your approach?

A: This reflects a culturally-framed explanatory model for his illness rather than a separate diagnosis, and should be explored respectfully rather than dismissed — understanding the family's beliefs (they arranged traditional rituals rather than seeking medical care) is important for engagement and for explaining, later, why ongoing pharmacological treatment matters despite that first apparent "recovery."

QHow would you address his poor drug compliance in the long term?

A: Structured psychoeducation for him and his parents about BAD as a recurrent, lifelong illness, identifying his own early warning signs of relapse (reduced sleep, overtalkativeness, overspending), and considering depot antipsychotic or closer community psychiatric nurse follow-up if oral compliance remains poor after this admission.
