Universal Psychiatric History Template

Long-case viva reference — structured order, key questions, and examiner-ready framing. Sources: OX Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry · HB Hanwella's Handbook of Clinical Psychiatry · BNF BNF-80 (side effects only)

1Introduction

  • Introduce yourself, confirm patient's name, age, and confirm consent to be examined/presented.
  • State who your informant is — usually the patient; if a relative/other informant was used, name them, their relationship, and duration of acquaintance OX.
  • Note the referral source and reason for referral (self, GP, ward, family) OX.
Viva framing: "This is Mrs/Mr X, a [age]-year-old [occupation], admitted/referred via [source], with informant being the patient herself/himself, who is a reliable/unreliable historian."

2Presenting Complaint (PC)

  • State in the patient's own words, with duration — e.g. "low mood for 3 months."
  • List each complaint separately with its own duration if there are several.
  • Always quantify duration precisely (days/weeks/months/years), not "a while."

3History of Presenting Complaint (HPC)

This is the diagnostic core of the interview OX. Structure chronologically from onset to now.

Points to cover

  • Onset — acute vs insidious; any identifiable trigger/stressor.
  • Course — continuous, episodic, fluctuating, deteriorating, improving.
  • Duration of each symptom individually.
  • Severity — functional impact on work, self-care, relationships (a useful severity marker: has the patient stopped working because of the illness?) HB.
  • Full symptom enquiry relevant to the suspected diagnosis (biological, cognitive, psychotic symptoms as relevant).
  • Pertinent negatives — explicitly record expected symptoms that are absent, e.g. no suicidal ideation in a depressed patient, no first-rank symptoms in a patient with delusions OX.
  • Precipitating/perpetuating/relieving factors.
  • Associated features — sleep, appetite, weight, biological functions.
  • Treatment so far — contact with services, medication trialled, doses, compliance, response, side effects OX.
  • Impact on functioning — occupational, social, self-care (GAF-type assessment) HB.
Viva tip: present HPC as a clear timeline — "symptoms began insidiously 3 months ago following X stressor, progressively worsening, now affecting occupational functioning as evidenced by absenteeism..." This demonstrates you understand severity grading, not just symptom listing.

4Family History

  • Parents and siblings — age, health status (or age/cause of death), occupation, personality, quality of relationship with patient OX.
  • Family psychiatric history — mood disorder, psychosis, suicide, substance misuse (specifically ask, not just "any mental illness") HB.
  • Family medical history relevant to presentation (e.g. epilepsy, thyroid disease, dementia).
  • If complex, draw/describe a family tree (genogram) OX.
  • Consanguinity and current household composition where relevant.

5Personal History

Variable in depth depending on age/diagnosis — more detailed in younger patients or where an early-life origin is suspected OX.

  • Birth & early development — antenatal/perinatal complications, developmental milestones.
  • Childhood — any prolonged separation from parents and reaction to it, emotional problems, serious childhood illness, family disruption.
  • Schooling — type, academic performance, relationships with peers/teachers, bullying (experienced or perpetrated), exclusions.
  • Occupational history — jobs held, duration, performance, reasons for leaving, current job satisfaction.
  • Relationship & sexual history — current partner (duration, quality), past relationships, marital history, attitude to sex/any difficulties.
  • Children — number, ages, health, who cares for them.
  • Social circumstances — accommodation, who patient lives with, financial situation, social support.
  • Substance use — alcohol, tobacco, illicit drugs: type, amount, duration, dependence features, problems caused.
  • Forensic history — arrests, convictions, imprisonment; nature and dangerousness of any offences OX.

6Past Psychiatric History

  • Previous episodes — nature, duration, similarity to current presentation.
  • Number of admissions, voluntary vs involuntary.
  • Treatments received — drug names, doses, duration, response, side effects, ECT if any.
  • Compliance history — has the patient defaulted from follow-up or stopped medication before?
  • Any past self-harm or suicide attempts — date, method, lethality, circumstances, outcome OX.
  • Longest period of wellness between episodes, and level of functioning during remission.

7Past Medical History

  • Chronic illnesses — diabetes, hypertension, epilepsy, thyroid disease, cardiac disease (relevant to both aetiology and drug choice).
  • Head injury, seizures, neurological illness — important in organic differential.
  • Current medication (psychiatric and non-psychiatric), including OTC and traditional/alternative remedies.
  • Allergies and past adverse drug reactions.

8Past Surgical History

  • Any operations, dates, complications.
  • Relevance to current presentation (e.g. thyroidectomy, bariatric surgery, any surgery with anaesthetic complications).
  • Hospitalisations in general.

9Premorbid Personality

Assessed from how the patient describes themselves before the onset of illness — asking "what were you like as a person before you became unwell?" Caution: it can be difficult to separate true premorbid personality from an emerging prodrome OX.

Domains to cover

  • Relationships — few/many friends, superficial or close, with same or opposite sex; relationships with colleagues/superiors.
  • Use of leisure time — hobbies, interests.
  • Predominant mood/emotional tone — anxious, despondent, optimistic, self-deprecating, overconfident, stable vs fluctuating OX.
  • Character traits — perfectionist, obsessional, isolated, impulsive, sensitive, controlling.
  • Attitudes and standards — moral/religious views, attitude to health.
  • "Ultimate concern" — what/who matters most in their life OX.

Viva tip: when asked "what was the premorbid personality?", answer with the domain-based description first (relationships, mood, traits), then explicitly state whether this fits a recognised personality type and how it relates aetiologically to the current diagnosis — this is exactly the link examiners are listening for OX.

10Risk Assessment

Always structured under three headings: risk to self, risk to others, and risk from others (vulnerability).

Risk to self

  • Suicidal ideation — passive ("wish I were dead") vs active (thoughts, plans, intent).
  • Any specific plan, means available, steps taken towards preparation (e.g. hoarding medication, writing a note).
  • Past attempts — method, lethality, circumstances of discovery, regret vs disappointment at survival.
  • Deliberate self-harm — method, frequency, function (e.g. affect regulation vs suicidal intent).
  • Impulsive/reckless behaviour secondary to illness — e.g. in mania: overspending, access to credit cards/bank accounts, resigning from job, sexual disinhibition HB.
  • Neglect of self-care, nutrition, or safety due to illness (e.g. in psychosis or severe depression).

Risk to others

  • Irritability, aggression, history of violence, current thoughts of harming a specific person.
  • Command hallucinations to harm others; delusions of a persecutory/jealous nature directed at an identifiable person.
  • Access to weapons or means.
  • Risk to dependents — children, elderly relatives under patient's care.
  • Forensic history of violence.
  • In mania specifically: disinhibited, argumentative, or aggressive behaviour, legal problems HB.

Risk from others (vulnerability)

  • Risk of exploitation, financial or sexual, due to illness (e.g. disinhibition in mania, cognitive impairment in dementia, negative symptoms in schizophrenia).
  • Domestic violence or abuse — current or historical.
  • Social isolation increasing vulnerability to neglect or exploitation.
  • Safeguarding concerns for dependent children/vulnerable adults in the household.
Viva tip: always finish risk assessment with an overall risk stratification statement — "overall risk to self is currently [low/moderate/high] given [specific factors], necessitating [level of observation/admission decision]." Examiners want to see synthesis, not just a list.

11Mental State Examination (MSE)

Covers signs and symptoms present at the time of interview, distinct from the history OX.

Appearance and Behaviour
  • General appearance — grooming, hygiene, dress (appropriate/dishevelled/bizarre/brightly coloured).
  • Eye contact — reduced (depression, psychosis), intense/hostile, avoidant.
  • Rapport — easy, guarded, suspicious, difficult to establish.
  • Psychomotor activity — retardation (depression) vs agitation; abnormal movements (extrapyramidal signs, tardive dyskinesia — relevant to antipsychotic side effects BNF).
  • Social disinhibition, over-familiarity, distractibility — difficulty sitting through interview, characteristic of mania HB.
  • Any evidence of self-neglect or catatonic features (posturing, waxy flexibility, mutism).
Speech
  • Rate — pressured/rapid (mania), slowed (depression, retardation).
  • Volume — loud, whispered, mute.
  • Quantity — talkative vs poverty of speech.
  • Form — spontaneous vs requiring prompting; tone and rhythm.
  • Note any dysarthria, neologisms, or thought disorder manifesting in speech (loosening of associations, word salad).
Mood and Affect
  • Subjective mood — ask directly, in the patient's own words ("how would you describe your mood?").
  • Objective affect — observed range, reactivity, and congruency with thought content.
  • Elated (mild/moderate/severe), depressed, anxious, irritable, labile (rapid swings, sometimes with both elation and depression) HB.
  • Note if mood is congruent or incongruent with described thought content.
  • Ask specifically about anhedonia, hopelessness, and diurnal variation where depression is suspected.
Thought
  • Form/stream — flight of ideas (mania), thought block, circumstantiality, tangentiality, loosening of associations.
  • Content — delusions (persecutory, grandiose, nihilistic, guilt, reference, jealousy), grandiose plans/schemes and increased self-esteem in mania HB; suicidal/homicidal ideation.
  • Obsessions and compulsions if relevant — thoughts, ruminations, doubts, impulses, obsessional phobias, rituals OX.
  • Possession of thought — thought insertion, withdrawal, broadcasting, passivity phenomena (note: rare in bipolar disorder, more supportive of schizophrenia when present) HB.
Perception
  • Hallucinations — modality (auditory most common; also visual, tactile, olfactory, gustatory), content (commanding, commentary, second/third person).
  • Illusions — misperception of a real stimulus.
  • Depersonalisation/derealisation.
  • Ask sensitively and non-leadingly — "do you ever hear/see things that others say they cannot?"
Cognition
  • Level of consciousness and orientation — time, place, person.
  • Attention and concentration — serial 7s, digit span, spelling WORLD backwards.
  • Memory — immediate registration, short-term recall (e.g. 3-word recall), long-term/autobiographical memory.
  • Do a detailed cognitive assessment (e.g. MMSE/MoCA) if organic aetiology is suspected HB.
  • Consider frontal/executive function testing if indicated (e.g. suspected frontotemporal dementia).
Insight
  • Does the patient believe they are unwell? Do they attribute symptoms to a psychiatric illness, a physical illness, or external causes?
  • Do they agree with the need for treatment and follow-up?
  • Grade descriptively rather than as simply "present/absent" — e.g. "partial insight: acknowledges low mood but attributes it entirely to marital problems and does not accept need for medication."
  • Insight is characteristically impaired in mania and psychosis HB.
Viva tip: present MSE fluently in the fixed order (Appearance & Behaviour → Speech → Mood → Thought → Perception → Cognition → Insight) even if you elicited findings out of sequence during the actual interview — examiners expect the standard structure OX.
