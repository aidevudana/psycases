Schizophrenia

F20 Oxford

Diagnostic requirement

At least one very clear symptom (usually two or more if less clear-cut) from groups (a)–(d), OR symptoms from at least two of the groups (e)–(h), clearly present for most of the time during a period of 1 month or more.

Groups (a)–(d) — First-rank type symptoms
  1. Thought echo, thought insertion or withdrawal, and thought broadcasting
  2. Delusions of control, influence, or passivity, clearly referred to body/limb movements or specific thoughts, actions, or sensations; delusional perception
  3. Hallucinatory voices giving a running commentary on the patient's behaviour, or discussing the patient among themselves, or other hallucinatory voices from some part of the body
  4. Persistent delusions of other kinds that are culturally inappropriate and completely impossible
Groups (e)–(h) — Need ≥2 present
  1. Persistent hallucinations in any modality, when accompanied either by fleeting/half-formed delusions without clear affective content, or persistent overvalued ideas, or occurring every day for weeks/months
  2. Breaks or interpolations in the train of thought, resulting in incoherence or irrelevant speech, or neologisms
  3. Catatonic behaviour — excitement, posturing, waxy flexibility, negativism, mutism, stupor
  4. 'Negative' symptoms — marked apathy, paucity of speech, blunting/incongruity of emotion, usually causing social withdrawal and lowered social performance (must not be due to depression or neuroleptics)

(i) A significant and consistent change in overall quality of personal behaviour — loss of interest, aimlessness, idleness, self-absorbed attitude, social withdrawal.

ICD-10 places weight on Schneider's first-rank symptoms and allows diagnosis after just 1 month (excludes prodrome) — unlike DSM-5, which requires 6 months and does not use subtypes. ICD-10 still recognises subtypes: paranoid, hebephrenic, catatonic, undifferentiated, residual, simple.

Differentials: brief psychotic disorder, delusional disorder, schizotypal disorder, schizoaffective disorder, psychotic depression, bipolar disorder, substance-induced psychosis, organic schizophrenia-like disorder (e.g. temporal lobe epilepsy).

Acute and Transient Psychotic Disorder

F23 Oxford

Acute onset psychosis with complete recovery within 2–3 months as the rule. May or may not be precipitated by a stressful life event.

  • Acute polymorphic psychotic disorder (with/without schizophrenia symptoms) — hallucinations, delusions, perceptual disturbance that are obvious but change rapidly in nature and extent, often with mood/motor changes (synonyms: bouffée délirante, cycloid psychosis)
  • Acute schizophrenia-like psychotic episode — meets symptom criteria for schizophrenia but lasts <1 month
  • Other acute psychotic episodes — residual cases not fitting the above

Corresponds loosely to DSM-5's brief psychotic disorder / schizophreniform disorder, though ICD-10 does not use "schizophreniform disorder" as a discrete category.

Schizoaffective Disorder

F25 Oxford

Diagnosis requires that definite schizophrenic and definite affective symptoms are both prominent and present simultaneously, or within a few days of each other. Should not be applied for an isolated symptom or two, or when assessment has been insufficiently detailed to identify a primary diagnosis.

Subclassified by the type of mood disturbance: depressive, manic, or mixed type.
Related: Postschizophrenic depression

Prominent depressive symptoms present for at least 2 weeks while some symptoms of schizophrenia (positive or negative) still remain — recognised in ICD-10 as a separate residual category from schizoaffective disorder.

Outcome: generally better than schizophrenia; negative symptoms rarely develop.

Hypomania

F30.0 Oxford Hanwella
  • Persistent mild elevation of mood for at least several days, with increased energy/activity and feelings of wellbeing
  • Increased sociability, talkativeness, overfamiliarity, increased sexual energy, decreased need for sleep
  • Mood disturbance is observable to others but not severe enough to cause marked impairment in social/occupational function, or to need hospital admission
  • No psychotic features

Manic Episode

F30 Oxford Hanwella
  • Mood elevated out of keeping with the individual's circumstances — from carefree joviality to almost uncontrollable excitement
  • Elation accompanied by increased energy → overactivity, pressure of speech, decreased need for sleep
  • Normal social inhibitions lost; attention cannot be sustained; marked distractibility
  • Self-esteem inflated; grandiose or over-optimistic ideas freely expressed (mood may instead be irritable)
  • Duration: episode should last at least 1 week, and be severe enough to disrupt ordinary work and social activities
Mania with psychotic symptoms

Grandiose ideas may develop into grandiose delusions; irritability/suspiciousness may cause persecutory delusions. Auditory hallucinations may occur. Sustained overactivity can lead to aggression/violence; neglect of eating, drinking, and hygiene can cause dangerous dehydration and self-neglect.

Differentials: schizophrenia (esp. if mixed features → schizoaffective), organic brain disease of the frontal lobes (tumour, HIV), amphetamine-induced excitement states.

Bipolar Affective Disorder

F31 Oxford Hanwella

Diagnosed only if the patient has had at least 2 episodes of mood disorder, of which at least one is hypomania, mania, or a mixed episode.

A single episode of mania/hypomania alone is classified as a manic episode — not bipolar disorder — under ICD-10 (contrast with DSM-5, where one manic episode suffices for Bipolar I).

Course specifierDescription
Currently hypomanic / manic / depressed / mixedBased on current episode
In remissionNo current episode
A mixed episode = criteria met for both a manic and a major depressive episode concurrently. Rapid cycling = 4 or more mood episodes within 12 months.

Depressive Episode

F32 Oxford Hanwella

Core (typical) symptoms — need for ≥2 weeks

  • Depressed mood
  • Loss of interest and enjoyment
  • Reduced energy → increased fatiguability and diminished activity

Other common symptoms

  1. Reduced concentration and attention
  2. Reduced self-esteem and self-confidence
  3. Ideas of guilt and unworthiness (even in mild episodes)
  4. Bleak, pessimistic view of the future
  5. Ideas or acts of self-harm or suicide
  6. Disturbed sleep
  7. Diminished appetite

If symptoms are particularly severe and of rapid onset, the diagnosis of severe depressive episode may be justified even if duration is <2 weeks.
Severe depression with psychotic features (F32.3)

Severe depressive criteria met, plus delusions, hallucinations, or depressive stupor.

  • Delusions of guilt: ideas of sin, poverty, or imminent disaster, responsibility assumed by the patient
  • Nihilistic delusions: internal organs destroyed, patient is dead, or has lost all possessions
  • Auditory hallucinations: usually defamatory/accusatory voices
  • Olfactory hallucinations: rotting filth or decomposing flesh
  • Severe psychomotor retardation may progress to depressive stupor
With somatic/melancholic features

At least 4 of: loss of interest/pleasure in usual activities; lack of emotional reactivity to normally pleasurable events; early-morning waking (≥2 hrs before usual time); depression worse in the morning; psychomotor agitation/retardation; marked loss of appetite; weight loss (≥5% body weight in the last month); marked loss of libido.

Recurrent Depressive Disorder

F33 Hanwella

Repeated episodes of mild, moderate, or severe depression occurring without episodes of mania. Recovery is usually complete between episodes.

FeatureDetail
Episode duration1–6 months untreated
Chronicity10–20% become chronic (>2 years)
Recurrence~85% experience more than one episode

Contrast with bipolar depression: bipolar depressive episodes tend to be longer and respond less well to treatment than unipolar (recurrent) depression.

Harmful Use

F1x.1 Oxford

A pattern of psychoactive substance use that is causing damage to health — the damage may be physical or mental.

It is possible to meet criteria for harmful use without meeting criteria for dependence — but the presence of dependence always implies harmful use.

Dependence Syndrome

F1x.2 Oxford Hanwella

Need ≥3 of the following, present together at some time during the previous year

  1. A strong desire or sense of compulsion to take the substance
  2. Difficulties in controlling substance-taking behaviour — in terms of onset, termination, or level of use
  3. A physiological withdrawal state when substance use has ceased/reduced — evidenced by the characteristic withdrawal syndrome, or use of the same/related substance to relieve or avoid withdrawal
  4. Evidence of tolerance — increased doses required to achieve effects originally produced by lower doses
  5. Progressive neglect of alternative pleasures/interests because of substance use, and increased time spent obtaining, using, or recovering from it
  6. Persisting with use despite clear evidence of overtly harmful consequences (physical or mental) — the person must be aware, or expected to be aware, of the harm
DSM-5 contrast: no separate "dependence" category — a single "substance use disorder" is graded mild (2–3 symptoms), moderate (4–5), or severe (≥6) out of 11 criteria.

Postpartum (Puerperal) Psychosis

No distinct code Oxford Hanwella
Important viva point: Neither ICD-10 nor DSM-5 has a specific diagnostic category for puerperal psychosis. It is coded under whichever underlying disorder best fits the clinical picture — most often bipolar affective disorder (F31) with mania/mixed features, a depressive episode (F32) with psychosis, or occasionally a schizophreniform/acute psychotic disorder (F23).

Clinical features supporting the label

  • Onset within the first 2–3 days of delivery, usually within 1–2 weeks; sudden onset, rapid deterioration
  • Three clinical pictures: delirium (now rarer, due to antibiotics reducing puerperal sepsis), mood disorder (mania, mixed, or depressive psychosis — now the commonest), or schizophreniform disorder (rare)
  • Poor sleep, restlessness, irritability, neglect of the baby, perplexity/fear
  • Delusions regarding the baby (deformed, dead, abnormal), delusions of guilt, persecutory delusions, auditory hallucinations
  • Thoughts of harming the baby driven by delusional beliefs — must be distinguished from the ego-dystonic intrusive thoughts of postnatal depression/OCD

Epidemiology: incidence 1–2 per 1000 births; up to 20% risk of puerperal relapse in women with pre-existing bipolar disorder; >75% good outcome; ≥35% risk of relapse after next delivery.
