1Introduction

Mrs. B.M.D. Dissanayaka, age unclear in scan, mother of two children, from Medawachchiya (place name unclear), who works as an accountant at a metal crusher company. Currently lives with her husband & 2 children.

2Presenting Complaint

She was brought by her family members, initially to Base Hospital Thabuttegama, due to a suicidal attempt — she jumped into a well — then was transferred to THA.

3History of Presenting Complaint

She is a known patient receiving psychiatric services since 2016. Since then, she has had low mood & feelings of unworthiness from time to time. This time, she attempted suicide on 23rd October. It occurred during a conflict with her husband & his brother, who had also become involved. She then left home and went to her parents' home with her younger son.

Her husband came to bring her back, but she refused, and there was another conflict; her husband then left home with the children. At that point she experienced a sudden impulse to end her life, as her husband told her to divorce him. She suddenly jumped into a well. However, her sister saw this, and with the help of family members and villagers, she was taken to Thabuttegama hospital & then transferred to THA, where she was admitted to ICU for 1 day, then a medical ward, and from there transferred to this ward.

Apart from this incident, she has had several previous episodes of suicidal attempts & deliberate self-harm. It all started around 2016, when she discovered her husband still had some relationship with his previous girlfriend. Since then, her illness began — she has had low mood, a sense of hopelessness & anxiety, and feels she is a burden to her family members. She has feelings of guilt, and a loss of interest & lack of enjoyment in activities that were previously pleasurable to her.

Now, most of the time she prefers to stay alone, with reduced interaction even with her family members. She also complains of reduced appetite and difficulty sleeping — she couldn't initiate sleep, and couldn't maintain sleep through the night.

She claims she still has ongoing stressors, as her husband's brother argues with them most of the time over land issues, which has led her to develop a pessimistic view of her children's future. After this incident, villagers spread rumours that she had an extramarital relationship, and she is now worried about that as well — she claims that because of this, she has wanted to end her life.

Over the last couple of years, her symptoms have gradually worsened, leading to several suicidal attempts within the last 3 months. Most of these acts followed an acute trigger. She previously had good drug compliance, and this is the first time she has been admitted to a psychiatric ward for in-patient management.

However, even with these stressors & events, she managed to attend her job, and maintained good personal care. She was able to carry out household activities and look after her children carefully, taking them to school every day — so there is a low risk of child neglect. She has a high suicidal risk as noted above, and a moderate risk to others, as she sometimes has impulsive urges towards her husband's brother & his wife, but has never acted on violence against them beyond verbal arguments.

She did not have past episodes of overtalkativeness, increased energy or elated mood. She denies any past history of substance abuse. No recent history of head trauma. She believes her thoughts are her own, and denies clearly hearing voices when no one else is nearby.

Severe depression — core diagnostic features

The Shorter Oxford Textbook characterises a depressive episode by persistent low mood, anhedonia, and reduced energy as core symptoms, plus associated features such as reduced concentration, low self-esteem, guilt, pessimism about the future, sleep and appetite disturbance, and suicidal ideation/acts — present for at least two weeks. "Severe" episodes typically show most of these symptoms markedly, with significant functional impact and often suicidal behaviour, as seen here. The absence of hallucinations or mood-congruent delusions (e.g. guilt-related delusions, nihilistic beliefs) is what keeps this "without psychotic symptoms" rather than psychotic depression.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Mood (Affective) Disorders — Depressive Episodes.

4Family History

5Personal History

Her childhood & development were uneventful. She was educated up to O/L, then married her husband, who is an air force officer, at the age of ~21 years. After marriage she completed her A/Ls and a diploma. She wanted to become a dancing teacher, but her husband didn't like the idea.

They have 2 children. Until last month she stayed at home looking after her two children, and recently went to work at a metal crusher company as an accountant. It was near home, so her husband didn't restrict her from working. At this time she denies any stressors at work, and works happily there.

However, she has ongoing stressors within the family due to recurrent conflicts with her husband's brother over land issues. She has a good relationship with her mother, father, siblings, husband & children. In her leisure time she used to dance & go out with her sisters, but she claims she now has poor interest in these activities. She denies past history of any other relationships except her husband, and no history of substance abuse.

6Past Psychiatric History

In 2016, during the antenatal period of her first pregnancy, she discovered her husband had a relationship with his previous girlfriend. She worried excessively about this, and developed low mood, loss of interest & enjoyment for several weeks. She sought medical attention and was diagnosed with depression. She was started on antidepressants, and her symptoms improved. Even with treatment, she had recurrent episodes, feeling somewhat better between episodes but not fully recovered.

Around this time, she developed a dependence on Clonazepam use. When she tried to stop, she developed withdrawal symptoms.

This is the first time she has been admitted to a psychiatric unit for in-patient management; during previous episodes she was managed in medical wards, and was on psychiatric clinic follow-up.

She has a past history of a total of 7 suicidal attempts. During the last 3 months, she made 5 attempts, but was not admitted to hospital for these. She attempted suicide twice by hanging herself, and on several occasions by self-ingestion of Clonazepam tablets (20–30 tablets, obtained separately from 3 different pharmacies). Her most recent attempt was this one, where she jumped into a well; just before that she had also tried to hang herself.

She doesn't have a history of violence, and has never acted violently against others.

7Past Medical / Surgical History

Unremarkable, apart from the Clonazepam dependence noted above.

8Premorbid Personality

Before this illness, she used to dance with her sister, which made her happy. She had good friends, and loved going out with them. She also actively participated in religious activities to relieve her stress. As mentioned above, she developed maladaptive coping strategies after her illness began, but before that, she used to tell everything to her sister to ventilate her feelings.

Sometimes, during a stressful event, she prefers to be socially isolated and stay in a quiet environment. In her leisure time she loves spending time with her children & dancing with her sister.

★ Risk Assessment

Risk of suicide — High. Most of her attempts were impulsive acts in response to a stressor — she has harmed herself even with a small stressor. She has searched for information online and collected medications from multiple sources. There are ropes and her own medications available at home, which she has previously used in attempts. She has ongoing stressors, as her husband's brother is in conflict with them over the land issue. Currently she has a stable job and good family support; however, social support is poor, as she doesn't interact much now, and tends to withdraw socially during stress. Overall, suicidal risk is high.

Risk to others / violence — Moderate. She has a past history of verbal conflicts with her husband's brother & his wife. Sometimes she has had thoughts of harming them, but has never acted on this — no history of physical violence. When stressed, she sometimes breaks household belongings, but later worries about this behaviour. No past history of physical violence against anyone, and no history of substance abuse. She has good family support but poor social support, good drug compliance, and is engaged with mental health services.

Risk of self-neglect — Low to Moderate. Despite her illness, she has been able to maintain good personal care; her overall hygiene is satisfactory. She has a good appetite now, but her sleep remains poor.

Risk to children — Moderate. She has never had thoughts of harming her children. Even with her illness, she takes good care of them, and takes them to school herself. She never argues with her husband in front of the children, and does not want them to know about her illness. Currently the children are doing well at school, with no complaints from teachers.

Suicide risk assessment in severe depression

The Shorter Oxford Textbook stresses that a history of multiple prior attempts (here, 7 in total, with 5 in the last 3 months) is one of the strongest predictors of future completed suicide, and that access to lethal means at home (medications, ropes) combined with poor social support and ongoing psychosocial stressors compounds this risk substantially. This is precisely why the case correctly grades suicidal risk "high" despite good family support and reasonable day-to-day functioning — risk assessment should weight the pattern of repeated impulsive attempts more heavily than current apparent stability.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Suicide and Attempted Suicide.

Depressive cognitions vs. mood-congruent delusions

The Shorter Oxford Textbook distinguishes ordinary depressive cognitions (pessimism, worthlessness, guilt, hopelessness — present here) from mood-congruent psychotic depression, where these themes become fixed, unshakeable delusional beliefs (e.g. delusions of guilt, poverty, or nihilism/Cotard's syndrome), sometimes with accompanying hallucinations. The explicit denial of delusions here, alongside intact insight, supports the "without psychotic symptoms" qualifier and has direct treatment implications (antidepressant monotherapy vs. antidepressant plus antipsychotic/ECT).

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Psychotic Depression.

Differential Diagnosis

  • Adjustment disorder
  • Somatoform disorder

Excluding the differentials

  • Adjustment disorder: typically a briefer, less severe reaction proportionate to an identifiable stressor, without the pervasive biological symptoms (sleep, appetite, energy) or repeated serious suicidal behaviour seen here.
  • Somatoform disorder: would be led by persistent physical symptoms without organic cause, rather than the mood, cognitive and biological depressive syndrome dominating this picture.
  • Depressive episode of BAD: requires evidence of a past manic/hypomanic episode — explicitly denied here (no history of elevated mood, overtalkativeness or increased energy).

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Differential Diagnosis of Depressive Disorders.

Summary

Mrs. B.M.D. Dissanayaka, mother of two children, is a diagnosed patient with severe depression without psychosis, this time admitted to a medical ward & transferred here following a suicidal attempt. She has had persistent low mood, lack of energy/fatiguability, reduced appetite & sleep disturbance for several months, with multiple suicidal attempts over the last 3 months. She denies feelings of guilt but consistently worries that she is a burden to her family and feels unworthy. She has recurrent conflicts with her husband's brother over land issues, and claims she persistently worries about these things. Since 2016, she has had 7 suicidal attempts in total, most of which were impulsive acts.

On examination, she had multiple self-harm scars on her forearm in retrospect; she was tearful, subjectively sad & objectively depressed. She was alert & well oriented, with good insight regarding her psychiatric illness.

Immediate Management

(1) Management setting. I would offer in-patient management, because: she is a diagnosed patient with severe depression; she was admitted following a suicidal attempt and still complains of suicidal thoughts; she has a past history of multiple suicidal attempts; and she has ongoing stressors within the family. I would inform staff about the patient and ensure the safety of both the patient and staff.

(2) Educating the patient and the family. I would educate them regarding the current condition, symptoms, treatment options, and prognosis, along with reasons for relapse and the importance of in-patient management.

(3) Assess the current condition. I would build a good rapport with the patient, then take a proper history and examination to assess the severity of illness, and assess her concerns directly. I would perform baseline investigations before starting medication — FBC, FBS, serum creatinine, serum electrolytes, LFTs.

I would recommend pharmacological and non-pharmacological management depending on current symptoms, and would trace her past medical records to know which drugs she was on and her response to previous medications.

(4) Pharmacological management. I would immediately stop Clonazepam, given her past history of dependence on it.

I would start her on a Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitor as first-line treatment — Fluoxetine 20mg oral (nocte), or Sertraline 50mg oral (nocte). I would avoid TCAs given they are toxic in overdose, and she has a significant history of self-harm/overdose. If an SSRI proves insufficiently effective, I would consider an SNRI such as Venlafaxine, starting at 37.5mg.

I would monitor her carefully with the help of nurses — current symptoms/compliance/side effects/behaviour/sleep & appetite/self-care/suicidal risk. As she has children at home, I would address any risk of child neglect that arises.

Why avoid TCAs here

The Shorter Oxford Textbook highlights that tricyclic antidepressants carry significant toxicity in overdose (cardiac arrhythmia, seizures), making them a poor choice for patients with active suicidal ideation or a history of impulsive self-poisoning — both present in this case. SSRIs are relatively much safer in overdose and are recommended as first-line for this reason as much as for their side-effect profile.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Antidepressant Drugs — Choice and Safety in Overdose.

Intermediate Management

I would arrange daily reviews to observe her improvement, monitor symptoms and make necessary dose adjustments; if response is poor, I would consider switching drugs or combination therapy.

Regular risk assessments — as she has suicidal thoughts, I would perform daily risk assessment during ward rounds.

Family meeting — I would arrange a family meeting to obtain collateral history, educate about the disease, identify the aetiology, and reinforce the importance of compliance. I would address any ongoing problems, such as the children's education and her overall level of functioning.

Non-pharmacological Management

Supportive psychotherapy — identification and resolution of the patient's current life difficulties, mainly through the social care worker, helping her cope with symptoms and address problems.

Cognitive behavioural therapy — in the cognitive component, she would be taught to identify maladaptive thinking patterns and challenge negative thoughts. The behavioural component encourages engagement in activities that improve mood and encourage further activity, helping her understand the connection between cognition and resulting distressing emotions.

Interpersonal therapy — to help manage difficulties in her relationships (notably the conflict with her husband's brother, and marital strain).

When symptoms are improving, I would arrange a parole leave. On her return, I would assess improvement and side effects of drugs, and consider any dose adjustments.

CBT in depression — the cognitive triad

The Shorter Oxford Textbook references Beck's cognitive model, where depression is maintained by a negative view of the self, the world, and the future (the "cognitive triad"). This patient's preoccupation with worthlessness (self), family conflict (world), and pessimism about her children's future (future) maps closely onto this triad, and is precisely the target of the cognitive restructuring component of CBT.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Depression.

Long-term Management

Goals: plan for discharge and follow-up care. Once acute symptoms have resolved and risks are low, I would consider discharging the patient and arrange follow-up. My discharge plan would include details of her assessment, the dose and frequency of treatment given in the ward, response to medications, and arrangement of a clinic visit for review (at THA or a base hospital, depending on medication availability).

I would inform the clinic and community psychiatric nurse about the patient. Before discharge, I would educate the patient & family regarding follow-up care, early warning signs of relapse, the importance of her physical health, good sleep and a relaxed mind, and the importance of treatment compliance and regular follow-up.

The main purpose of follow-up is to maintain the achieved improvement and prevent relapse. At each follow-up visit I would assess: extent and severity of symptoms; risk assessment; drug compliance; side effects of medications; ongoing psychological treatment; and level of functioning.

I would educate her that it typically takes about 2–3 months to become completely free of symptoms, and that she would need to continue treatment for a further 6 months to prevent relapse.

Duration of antidepressant treatment

Consistent with the Shorter Oxford Textbook, antidepressants should typically be continued for at least 6 months after symptom remission (longer after recurrent or severe episodes, as here) to reduce the risk of early relapse, with gradual tapering rather than abrupt discontinuation once treatment is eventually stopped.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Continuation and Maintenance Treatment of Depression.

SSRIs (e.g. Fluoxetine, Sertraline)

  • Anxiety, GI disturbance, dry mouth, sexual dysfunction
  • Uncommon: menstrual irregularities, tremors, alopecia/galactorrhoea, serotonin syndrome, thrombocytopenia

TCAs (avoided here, but relevant for viva)

  • Anticholinergic syndrome: dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, urinary retention
  • Drowsiness, weight gain, gynaecomastia, hypertension
  • Alopecia, agranulocytosis, anxiety, arrhythmia (danger in overdose)

Serotonin syndrome — recognition

The Shorter Oxford Textbook describes serotonin syndrome as a triad of neuromuscular abnormalities (tremor, clonus, hyperreflexia), autonomic instability (hyperthermia, tachycardia, sweating), and altered mental state (agitation, confusion), typically arising from combining serotonergic agents (e.g. SSRI + MAOI, or SSRI + tramadol). Given this patient's history of collecting/self-administering medications from multiple sources, clear counselling about avoiding other serotonergic drugs and over-the-counter interactions is particularly important here.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Antidepressant Drug Interactions and Serotonin Syndrome.

QWhy is this graded a severe rather than moderate depressive episode?

A: All three core symptoms are present (persistent low mood, anhedonia, reduced energy) alongside multiple additional biological and cognitive features (sleep and appetite disturbance, guilt/worthlessness, hopelessness), together with repeated serious suicidal acts — the combination of pervasive core symptoms plus high-risk behaviour is what pushes this to severe, even though she has maintained her job.

QWhy "without psychotic symptoms"?

A: She denies hallucinations and does not have fixed, unshakeable delusions of guilt, poverty, or nihilism — her negative cognitions (worthlessness, being a burden) remain ordinary depressive thinking rather than delusional in intensity, and her insight is preserved throughout.

QWhy choose an SSRI over a TCA here specifically?

A: TCAs are significantly more toxic in overdose (cardiac arrhythmia, seizures), which matters greatly for a patient with seven previous suicidal attempts, several of them by self-poisoning. SSRIs are much safer in overdose, making them the appropriate first-line choice here as much for safety as for side-effect profile.

QWhy was Clonazepam stopped on admission, and is abrupt discontinuation a concern?

A: She has an established dependence on Clonazepam, having previously self-administered 20–30 tablets sourced from multiple pharmacies during suicide attempts — continuing it perpetuates that risk. In a genuinely dependent patient, abrupt cessation does carry a withdrawal risk, so this is a reasonable follow-up question to flag: a supervised, monitored taper may be safer than an abrupt stop, even though removing access quickly is also a safety priority here.

QHow do you reconcile her "high" suicide risk with her currently good functioning at work and with her children?

A: The textbook principle is that a pattern of repeated impulsive attempts (seven in total, five in the last three months) is one of the strongest predictors of future risk, and this static factor should be weighted more heavily than her current apparent stability. Good day-to-day functioning does not offset a well-established pattern of acting impulsively under stress.

QWhat is the "cognitive triad" and how does it map onto this patient?

A: Beck's cognitive triad describes a negative view of the self, the world, and the future. Here: self (feelings of worthlessness and being a burden), world (ongoing family conflict with her husband's brother), and future (pessimism about her children's prospects) — this mapping is precisely what cognitive restructuring in CBT would target.

QWhy is a depressive episode of Bipolar Affective Disorder excluded here?

A: She has never had a past episode of elevated mood, overtalkativeness, decreased need for sleep, or increased energy — the diagnostic requirement for bipolar depression is evidence of at least one past hypomanic or manic episode, which is explicitly absent from this history.

QWhy is serotonin syndrome a particular concern for this patient?

A: She has a demonstrated pattern of sourcing medications from multiple different pharmacies without full disclosure. This raises the risk of unknowingly combining serotonergic drugs (for example, an SSRI with another serotonergic agent obtained elsewhere), so clear counselling about drug interactions is especially important in her case, not just generic advice.

QHow long would you continue her antidepressant, and why?

A: At least 6 months after full symptom remission, and likely longer given this is a recurrent, severe episode with a long illness history since 2016 — continuing beyond the minimum reduces the risk of early relapse, with any eventual discontinuation done gradually rather than abruptly.

QWhy is risk to her children graded moderate despite her currently good caregiving?

A: She actively protects her children now — taking them to school herself and shielding them from marital conflict — which keeps direct risk from being high. But her demonstrated impulsivity and high suicide risk mean there is an inherent indirect risk (for example, an attempt occurring at home, or reduced capacity to care for them during a future severe relapse), which is why the risk is not simply graded low.
