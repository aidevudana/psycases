3History of Presenting Complaint

He is a known patient who has been receiving psychiatric services for the last 2 years, but defaulted clinic follow-up. This is the 2nd day after admission. The day before admission, while at home, he had a conflict with his wife regarding his alcohol consumption. The patient accepted that he was under the influence of alcohol at the time. There was only a verbal argument — no physical violence, according to the patient. His wife then called the police, and he was brought to the ward. After admission, several blood investigations were done, and he was given some IV and oral medications.

According to the patient, he started drinking alcohol at the age of 16 years, initially due to the influence of his friends. During that period he consumed alcohol occasionally with friends, about 750ml per week. Other than arrack, he has also consumed non-distilled beverages & beer from time to time.

Since then, the amount of alcohol he consumed — alone or sometimes with friends — increased, along with occasional beer. Initially, there was no evidence of violence or any abnormal behaviour towards others following alcohol consumption.

With time, he started drinking not only on weekends but also during the day. He progressed to daily drinking, where he needed alcohol to continue his usual activities. He describes strong cravings to consume alcohol, and that once he started drinking he could not stop. Over time he developed an urge to drink alcohol in the morning. He also had evidence of tolerance, needing a higher amount of alcohol than previously to achieve the same effect, and complained of loss of pleasure during this period.

Whenever he couldn't consume alcohol for a day, he developed tremors, loss of appetite & sleep disturbances; sometimes there was associated vomiting as well. Despite awareness of the negative consequences of alcohol, he continued to use it.

He denies past history of severe intoxication, no history of withdrawal seizures or acute confusional state in the past, and no past history of loss of consciousness with ataxia or gait impairment. Other than alcohol, he denies any other illicit drug or psychoactive substance abuse.

Medical: He had burning-type epigastric pain with regurgitation for the last few months, treated as gastritis. He currently complains of nausea, reduced appetite, and sleep disturbance. He denies serious medical problems such as myocardial infarction.

Psychiatric: He denies any evidence of a delusional disorder, no morbid jealousy, no suspiciousness regarding his wife. He denies related memory impairment. He believes his thoughts are his own, and denies hearing voices when he is alone. He denies loss of interest & lack of enjoyment, and denies suicidal thoughts or ideation.

Financial: He is the breadwinner of the family. He admits he spends part of his salary on alcohol; however, he was able to fulfil his family's needs.

He has a past history of drink-driving, but has never had an accident. He has recurrent conflicts with his wife because of his drinking, with no history of domestic violence. He denies any behavioural problems in his children, though he acknowledges this could negatively impact their mental health. He denies poor performance at work even under the influence of alcohol, and no history of work-related accidents.

He doesn't have any suicidal thoughts, no past history of violence, and no history of road traffic accidents.

Alcohol dependence syndrome — ICD criteria

The Shorter Oxford Textbook summarises the dependence syndrome (ICD) as requiring several of: compulsion/craving to drink, difficulty controlling drinking, physiological withdrawal state, tolerance, progressive neglect of alternative pleasures, and persisting use despite awareness of harm. This history documents all of these clearly — craving, loss of control ("couldn't stop"), a withdrawal syndrome (tremor, sleep/appetite disturbance, vomiting), tolerance, and continued use despite gastritis and family conflict — giving a textbook basis for the diagnosis.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Alcohol Misuse and Dependence.

Risk assessment in alcohol dependence

Per the Shorter Oxford Textbook, risk assessment in alcohol misuse should routinely cover self-harm/suicide, violence (particularly domestic), accidents (road traffic and occupational), risk to dependants, and self-neglect/nutritional risk (notably thiamine deficiency), since alcohol dependence carries elevated risk across all these domains even without a single dominant "red flag." This case illustrates a fairly typical moderate-risk profile spread across several domains rather than one severe risk, which shapes the choice of a cautious but not maximally restrictive management setting.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Alcohol Misuse — Risk and Complications.

Stages of change (Prochaska & DiClemente)

The Shorter Oxford Textbook frames motivation to change substance use along the transtheoretical "stages of change" model: pre-contemplation → contemplation → preparation → action → maintenance (± relapse, cycling back). Documenting that this patient is in the contemplation stage — aware of the problem, weighing pros/cons, not yet fully committed to action — directly determines the choice of motivational interviewing as the primary psychological technique in his management plan, rather than more action-oriented relapse-prevention work alone.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Models of Behaviour Change in Addiction.

Summary

Mr. Prasad Siripala, 40 years old, chronic alcohol consumer, presented to the ward following a conflict with his wife while under the influence of alcohol. He has been under psychiatric services for the last 2 years for alcohol dependence, had a 6-month period of abstinence, then relapsed — this time also with withdrawal symptoms such as bilateral tremors, nausea, loss of appetite and sleep disturbance. Management plan: deciding treatment setting → deciding treatment goals → detoxification of the dependent patient → relapse prevention.

Treatment Setting

I would consider admitting this patient for in-ward management, as he is an alcohol-dependent patient with poor social & family support. He did not have severe withdrawal symptoms such as delirium tremens or other complications like withdrawal seizures, hepatic illness, comorbid psychiatric illness, or liver dysfunction — but the poor social support and history of relapse justify inpatient detoxification.

Acute Management — Detoxification

I would start a tapering dose of Chlordiazepoxide. Before starting treatment, I would perform baseline investigations including FBC, FBS/RBS, serum electrolytes, and liver function tests.

I would assess the severity of alcohol withdrawal using the CIWA-Ar score (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol scale). Patients with a CIWA score below the mild threshold do not need pharmacological treatment; moderate-to-severe withdrawal can be treated with a tapering-dose or titrating-dose regime.

Benzodiazepines (such as Chlordiazepoxide) have a long half-life and low addictive potential, making them the treatment of choice. Why thiamine before glucose

A frequently tested point in the Shorter Oxford Textbook: administering glucose to a thiamine-deficient patient can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy, as glucose metabolism consumes remaining thiamine stores. IV thiamine should therefore always be given before, or alongside, glucose-containing fluids in a patient at risk of alcohol-related thiamine deficiency, with caution for anaphylaxis during IV administration.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Alcohol Withdrawal and Wernicke-Korsakoff Syndrome.

Managing Complications

I would correct hypoglycaemia if present on investigation. If the patient develops delirium tremens, I would liaise with the general medical team, manage him in a calm & safe environment, and he may need higher doses of Chlordiazepoxide for about 2 weeks.

I would give IV fluids, correct electrolytes, hydrate the patient, and provide adequate nutrition, then monitor his symptoms. I would perform liver function tests to differentiate delirium tremens from delirium due to liver failure, and arrange medical referral if hepatic dysfunction is present.

Management of Wernicke's encephalopathy: prophylaxis with oral thiamine 100mg daily during detoxification. If symptomatic, IV thiamine 200–500mg 3 times per day for 3 days, then 250–300mg/day IV for 5 days, taking precautions for the risk of anaphylaxis with IV thiamine.

Patient and family education: I would educate the family regarding the patient's condition, aetiology, and treatment options, obtain collateral history to understand the relapse cycle, and stress the importance of treatment adherence to prevent relapse.

Intermediate Management

I would conduct daily ward rounds to assess improvement of his withdrawal symptoms, with regular CIWA reassessments, and address any ongoing problems, as the patient could not attend his job during his ward stay.

Non-pharmacological Management

I would focus on helping the patient make a change and maintain abstinence. Psychological treatment options include:

  • Motivational interviewing
  • Cognitive behavioural therapy
  • Psychoeducation
  • Social and interpersonal skills training
  • Relapse prevention with exposure
  • Problem-solving skills

Motivational interviewing: educate about the cycle of behaviour change. As he is in the contemplation stage, I would provide information about alcohol-related harm and discuss the advantages of reducing consumption, using open-ended questions, appreciating positive things about the patient, and directing him towards "change talk" rather than "sustain talk." I would instruct him to discard remaining alcohol at home, and try to avoid people who encourage him to drink.

Cognitive behavioural therapy:

  • Management of urge — convince the patient this is short-lasting.
  • Cue cards — cards written with adverse past experiences due to alcohol.
  • Urge surfing — train the patient's mind to move through the urge without trying to suppress it.
  • Distraction — listening to music, walking, gardening, or talking to family.
  • Habit break-up plan — identify high-risk situations (weddings, parties, socialising with friends, following an argument) and collaboratively select alternative coping strategies for each.

Family meeting: to appreciate the patient's abstinence, advise family members on how to help suppress his urge to drink, and encourage his continued participation in rehabilitation.

Cognitive therapy: identify dysfunctional beliefs and use cognitive restructuring, challenging his beliefs, and building emotional-regulation skills.

Problem-solving skills / assertiveness training: if a relapse occurs, he should leave the place where he consumed alcohol. If it happens at home, remaining alcohol should be disposed of, and a support person identified for emergencies.

Social management: social behaviour and network therapy — engaging family and friends, social skills acquisition, strategies for dealing with crises, and pleasant social activities as an alternative to drinking. Drink-refusal skills training — assertiveness, and choosing non-alcoholic drinks socially.

Motivational interviewing principles

The Shorter Oxford Textbook describes motivational interviewing as a collaborative, non-confrontational style that works with the patient's own ambivalence, using open questions, affirmations, reflective listening, and summarising (OARS) to elicit the patient's own "change talk" rather than imposing advice — consistent with the approach outlined here, appropriately matched to a patient in the contemplation stage.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Psychological Treatments for Substance Misuse.

Long-term Management

Once acute symptoms have improved, I would consider discharging the patient and arrange a follow-up plan for relapse prevention. I would start pharmacological therapy for relapse prevention — choosing from Acamprosate, Disulfiram, Naltrexone, or Topiramate, depending on suitability.

At follow-up visits I would encourage him to continue his lifestyle changes, monitor him using his habit-breaking plan, and advise him that if he does relapse, he should come and seek medical attention promptly, as relapse is a normal part of the recovery process.

Acamprosate (GABA agonist / glutamate antagonist)

  • 333mg tab tds, for 6–12 months. Contraindicated in severe renal impairment.
  • Side effects: abdominal pain, diarrhoea, sexual dysfunction, drowsiness.

Disulfiram (Acetaldehyde dehydrogenase inhibitor)

  • Start 100–200mg/day; must start only when blood alcohol level is zero for ≥24h.
  • Contraindicated in recent heart disease, psychosis, suicidal attempts, patients on antihypertensives, or hepatic failure.
  • Side effects: breath odour, metallic taste, decreased libido.

Naltrexone (opioid receptor antagonist — reduces craving)

  • Start 25mg/day, increase to 50mg/day; start only after achieving abstinence.
  • Side effects: GI upset, opioid withdrawal (if opioid-dependent), lethargy, sexual dysfunction, anxiety, alopecia.

Topiramate

  • 25–50mg bd.
  • Side effects: mood disorder, anaemia, constipation.

Choosing a relapse-prevention agent

The Shorter Oxford Textbook notes Acamprosate and Naltrexone as first-line anti-craving agents with the best evidence base, Disulfiram as an aversive agent requiring high motivation and careful cardiac/psychiatric screening given its strict abstinence requirement and interaction risks, and Topiramate as an option with emerging evidence, particularly where the licensed options are unsuitable or ineffective. Renal and hepatic function, cardiac history, and current mental state should all be checked before starting any of these agents.

Cf. Shorter Oxford Textbook of Psychiatry, chapter on Pharmacological Relapse Prevention in Alcohol Dependence.

QWhich ICD dependence criteria are demonstrated in this history?

A: Strong desire/craving to drink, difficulty controlling consumption ("once he started drinking he could not stop"), a physiological withdrawal state (tremor, poor sleep, vomiting on abstinence), tolerance (needing more to achieve the same effect), progressive neglect of alternatives, and continued use despite clear harm (gastritis, family conflict) — enough to satisfy the dependence syndrome well beyond the minimum threshold.

QWhy is his withdrawal classified as "uncomplicated"?

A: He has tremor, nausea, poor appetite and sleep disturbance, but no seizures, no delirium tremens, and no alcoholic hallucinosis — either now or in his past history. Complicated withdrawal specifically refers to those additional features, none of which are present here.

QWhy must thiamine be given before (or with) glucose in an at-risk patient?

A: Glucose metabolism consumes thiamine as a cofactor. In a thiamine-deficient patient, giving glucose first can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's encephalopathy — so thiamine should always be given before or alongside any glucose-containing fluid in alcohol-dependent patients at nutritional risk.

QWhat is the CIWA-Ar scale used for?

A: It grades the severity of alcohol withdrawal symptoms and guides whether pharmacological treatment is needed at all, and if so, whether a fixed tapering-dose or a titrating-dose benzodiazepine regime is appropriate — low scores may not need drug treatment, while higher scores warrant more intensive dosing.

QWhy is he described as being in the "contemplation" stage, and how does that change your management?

A: He is aware his drinking is a problem and is willing to take medication, but has not yet committed to concrete action — this is the contemplation stage of Prochaska and DiClemente's model. It's why motivational interviewing (exploring ambivalence, eliciting his own "change talk") is the primary psychological technique here, rather than pure relapse-prevention work suited to someone already in the action stage.

QWhy admit him at all, given his withdrawal is uncomplicated?

A: He lacks strong social support at home for a safe outpatient detox and has a documented history of relapse after previous periods of abstinence. Admission indications are not limited to medical severity of withdrawal — poor social support and relapse risk are independently sufficient reasons for inpatient detoxification.

QIf he were only intermittently abstinent and might drink again, would you still choose Disulfiram?

A: Disulfiram requires strict abstinence, informed consent, and high motivation, because drinking on it causes a dangerous reaction — it is a poor choice for someone likely to relapse unpredictably. Acamprosate or Naltrexone are more forgiving anti-craving options in a patient whose commitment to total abstinence is less certain.

QWhy check liver function tests before starting Disulfiram?

A: Disulfiram is contraindicated in hepatic failure and can itself cause hepatotoxicity, so baseline liver function must be confirmed adequate before starting, and monitored periodically thereafter.

QWhat long-term physical complications of alcohol use should be screened for at follow-up?

A: Liver disease (fatty liver, hepatitis, cirrhosis), peripheral neuropathy, cardiomyopathy, pancreatitis (relevant here given his gastritis-type symptoms), and ongoing nutritional/thiamine deficiency — a useful checklist to mention even though none are yet clinically apparent in this case.

QWhy is his risk of accidents and violence graded moderate rather than low?

A: There is real exposure — a past history of drink-driving, work as a mason involving heights and sharp tools, and recurrent verbal conflicts with his wife — but no actual injuries, accidents, or physical violence have occurred. This is a dynamic risk based on exposure and pattern of behaviour, rather than a completed harmful event, which is why it sits at moderate rather than low or high.
